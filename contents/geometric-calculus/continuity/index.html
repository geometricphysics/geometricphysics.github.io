Functions, Limits and Continuity

The mathematical notation for a limit of a function, what it means logically, and what it means informally, are built systematically making it easier to remember what the notation means.

Deconstructing the Limit of a Function Notation

Notation Logical Meaning Informal Menaing
$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{x \rightarrow argval} f(x) & = & value \end{eqnarray} $ The logical statement is built from right to left by defining
  1. What the function does $f(x) = value$.
  2. The assumption about the argument $x \rightarrow argval$.
  3. The existance of value relating to the assumption.
  4. An statement about arbitrary choice of precision for the function value.
 Meaning in words

In the definitions below, $stmt_{1} \Rightarrow stmt_{2}$ is used. This is normally written as $stmt_{2}$ whenever $stmt_{1}$.

Definitions

Let the value $f(x)$ be defined and single valued for all values of $x$ near $x = a$ with the possible exception of $x = a$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) & = & L \end{eqnarray} $ $ \forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0 \colon 0 < |x - a| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x) - \epsilon| < L $ $L$ is the limit of $f(x)$, as $x$ approaches $a$.
$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{x \rightarrow a^{+}} f(x) & = & L \end{eqnarray} $ $ \forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0 \colon 0 < x - a < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x) - \epsilon| < L $ $L$ is the limit of $f(x)$, as $x$ approaches $a$ from the right hand (positive) side.
$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{x \rightarrow a^{-}} f(x) & = & L \end{eqnarray} $ $ \forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0 \colon - \delta < x - a < 0 \Rightarrow |f(x) - \epsilon| < L $ $L$ is the limit of $f(x)$, as $x$ approaches $a$ from the left hand (negative) side.
$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) & = & \infty \end{eqnarray} $ $ \forall M > 0, \exists \delta > 0 \colon 0 < |x - a| < \delta \Rightarrow f(x) > M $ $f(x)$ tends to infinity as $x$ approaches $a$.
$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) & = & - \infty \end{eqnarray} $ $ \forall N < 0, \exists \delta > 0 \colon 0 < |x - a| < \delta \Rightarrow f(x) < N $ $f(x)$ tends to negative infinity as $x$ approaches $a$.
$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} f(x) & = & L \end{eqnarray} $ $ \forall \epsilon > 0, \exists M > 0 \colon x > M \Rightarrow |f(x) - \epsilon| < L $ $L$ is the limit of $f(x)$, as $x$ tends to infinity.
$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{x \rightarrow - \infty} f(x) & = & L \end{eqnarray} $ $ \forall \epsilon > 0, \exists N < 0 \colon x < N \Rightarrow |f(x) - \epsilon| < L $ $L$ is the limit of $f(x)$, as $x$ tends to negative infinity.

Continuity

When we say that a function $f$ with argument $x$ is continuous at $x=a$ we mean

$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x) & = & f(a) \end{eqnarray} $
