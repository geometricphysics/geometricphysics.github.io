Derivatives

The Increment

The increment given to a variable is

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Delta x & = & (x + \Delta x) - x \end{eqnarray} $

The Derivative

Let $f$ be a function of $x$ only. The derivative of $f$ at $x$ is defined as

$ \begin{eqnarray} f'(x) & = & \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x} \end{eqnarray} $

if the limit exists. i.e.

$\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ such that $|\frac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x}| < \epsilon$ whenever $0 < |x + \Delta x| < \delta$

A function is called differentiable at the point $x$ if it has a derivative at this point. i.e. $f'(x)$ exists.

We are implicitly assuming that $f(x)$ is defined.

If $f$ has a derivative at $x$, $f$ must be continuous at $x$. Proof:

$ \begin{eqnarray} f(x + \Delta x) - f(x) & = & \frac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x} \cdot \Delta x \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Then
$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} [f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)] & = & \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x} \cdot \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} \Delta x \\ & = & f'(x) \cdot 0 \\ & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

Hence

$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} f(x + \Delta x) & = & f(x) \end{eqnarray} $ showing that $f$ is continuous at $x$.

Differentiability in an Interval

If a function has a derivative at all points in an interval, we say that the function is differentiable in the interval.

Product Rule

Let $u = u(x)$, and $v=v(x)$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d}{d x} u v & = & u \frac{d v}{d x} + \frac{d u}{d x} v \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Chain Rule

Let $y = y(u)$, and $u = u(x)$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d y}{d x} & = & \frac{d y}{d u} \cdot \frac{d u}{d x} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Differentials

Suppose $y = f(x)$ and that $\Delta x = dx$ is an increment given to $x$. Then

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Delta y & = & f(x + \Delta x) - f(x) \\ & = & f'(x) \Delta x + \epsilon \Delta x \\ & = & f'(x) dx + \epsilon dx \\ \end{eqnarray} $

where $\epsilon \rightarrow 0$ as $\Delta x \rightarrow 0$.

The differential of y or the principal part of $\Delta y$ is defined to be

$ \begin{eqnarray} dy & = & f'(x) dx \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

It should be noted that the quantities $dx$ and $dy$ are not the limits of $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ as $\Delta x \rightarrow 0$.

Applying this definition for the differential of $y$ and $x$, we can write

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{dy}{dx} & = & f'(x) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Rolle's theorem

Between two values of a variable where a function is zero, there exists a point where the derivative is zero.
