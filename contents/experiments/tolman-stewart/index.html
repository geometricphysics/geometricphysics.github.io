Tolman & Stewart (1916)

Introduction

A direct measurement of the charge to mass ratio of an electron.

Theory

  • Inertia of the free electrons is responsible for a current.
  • Joule heating causes the current to decay over time.
  • Conservation of energy provides the dynamical equations.

The kinetic energy $K$ of the electrons due to their angular velocity $\omega$ and moment of inertial $I$ is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} K & = & \frac{1}{2} I \omega^2 \end{eqnarray} $

Let $m$ be the mass of the electron and $n_e$ the free electron density. For a circular coil of wire of $N$ turns of major radius $a$ and cross-sectional area $A \ll a$, the moment of inertia is

$ \begin{eqnarray} I & = & m n_e A (2 \pi a) N a^2 \end{eqnarray} $

The current decays because of Joule heating. Let the current in the loop be $J$ and the resistance of the circuit be $R$ then

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d K}{d t} & = & I \omega \frac{d \omega}{d t} & = & J^2 R \end{eqnarray} $

From the geometry of the system

$ \begin{eqnarray} J & = & e n_e v A = e n_e a \omega A \end{eqnarray} $

Hence

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{e}{m} J dt & = & \frac{2 \pi N a^2}{R} d \omega \end{eqnarray} $

Integrating

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{e}{m} & = & \frac{2 \pi N a^2}{Q R} \omega \end{eqnarray} $

Orders of Magnitude

If we assume that most of the resistance $R$ of the circuit is die to the coil of wire then if the conductivity of the wire is $\sigma$

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{1}{R} & = & 2 \pi a N \sigma \end{eqnarray} $

Giving

$ \begin{eqnarray} Q & = & (2 \pi a N)^2 a \sigma \omega \frac{m}{e} \end{eqnarray} $

  • The coil rotates at around 5000 rpm
  • The radius of the coil $a$ is approx 0.25m
  • The length of the wire $L = 2 \pi a N$ is around 500m
  • The conductivity $\sigma$ of copper is $10^{-7} \Omega^{-1} m^{-1}$
  • The electron mass is $0.911 \times 10^{-31}$ kg
  • The electron charge is $1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C

This gives a total charge of around $10^{-10}$ C.
