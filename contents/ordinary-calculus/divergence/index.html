Divergence

Definition and Meaning

Let $\vec{J} : (vector) \rightarrow vector$ be a vector-valued field defined over some region. We wish to compute the total flux of $\vec{J}$ out of a volume $V$ enclosed by a boundary $\partial V$ with an outward normal. If divide this total flux by $V$ and take the limit as $V$ tends to zero we obtain the outward flux of $\vec{J}$ per unit volume called the divergence of $\vec{J}$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} div \vec{J} & = & \lim_{V \rightarrow 0} \frac{\int \vec{J} \cdot d \vec{A}}{V} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

This is a coordinate free expression that shows that $div \vec{J}$ is a scalar field.

Expression for $div \vec{J}$

We can obtain an expression for $div \vec{J}$ by considering first the flux of $\vec{J}$ over a differential rectangular parallelepiped.

$ \begin{eqnarray*} \int_{dV} \vec{J} \cdot d \vec{A} & = & \vec{J}(x+dx,y,z) \cdot (\hat{i}) dy dz + \vec{J}(x,y,z) \cdot (-\hat{i}) dy dz + \cdots \\ & = & \frac{\partial J_{x}}{\partial x} dx dy dz + \cdots \\ & = & (\frac{\partial J_{x}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial J_{y}}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial J_{z}}{\partial z}) dV \\ & = & (div \vec{J}) dV \end{eqnarray*} $.

Hence

$ \begin{eqnarray*} div \vec{J} & = & \frac{\partial J_{x}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial J_{y}}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial J_{z}}{\partial z} \end{eqnarray*} $.

Notation

It is common to see the notation $\nabla \cdot \vec{J}$ used for the divergence of $\vec{J}$, where

$ \begin{eqnarray*} \nabla & = & \hat{i} \frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \hat{j} \frac{\partial}{\partial y} + \hat{k} \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \end{eqnarray*} $.

Although this notation is suggesting that the operator $\nabla$ (del) is a hybrid scalar-product and partial derivative operator, the meaning of the notation $\nabla \cdot \vec{J}$ is the expression for $div \vec{J}$.

Gauss's Theorem

We can immediately obtain a relation between the surface integral of a vector and the volume integral of the divergence of the vector

$ \begin{eqnarray*} \int_{\partial V} \vec{J} \cdot d \vec{S} & = & \int_{V} \nabla \cdot \vec{J} dV \end{eqnarray*} $.
