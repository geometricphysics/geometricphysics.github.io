Partial differentiation

Generalizing scalar functions to several variables

Consider the function $\phi$ which depends on $n$ variables $x^{1}, \ldots , x^{n}$.

I'm using superscripts as variable indices because the most common usage for functions of several variables will be to represent fields as mappings from coordinates to a value.

By the shorthand notation $\phi(x^{i})$ we mean the value

$ \begin{eqnarray} \phi(x^{1}, \ldots , x^{n}) \end{eqnarray} $.

The Partial Derivative

The partial derivative is a generalization of the single variable case

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x^{k}} (x^{i}) & = & \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{\phi(x^{1}, \ldots , x^{k}+h, \ldots , x^{n}) - \phi(x^{1}, \ldots , x^{k}, \ldots , x^{n})}{h} \end{eqnarray} $.

Notice that the formal way to write the left hand side involves specifying where the partial derivative is evaluated. It's common to see the call to the function omitted.

The Total Differental

When all $n$ variables are allowed to change the total differential of $\phi$ is defined as

$ \begin{eqnarray} d \phi & = & \phi(x^{i}+dx^{i}) - \phi(x^{i}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

By applying the definition of the partial derivative and "adding zero" $n$ times we prove the following theorem

$ \begin{eqnarray} d \phi & = & \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x^{i}} dx^{i} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

The Chain Rule for partial differentiation

Suppose $x^{i} = x^{i}(q^{j})$. Then the change in $x^{i}$ resulting from a change in $q^{j}$ is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} d x^{i} & = & \frac{\partial x^{i}}{\partial q^{j}} d q^{j} \end{eqnarray} $.

Suppose $x^{i} = x^{i}(q^{j})$. Then $ \begin{eqnarray} d x^{i} & = & \frac{\partial x^{i}}{\partial q^{j}} d q^{j} \end{eqnarray} $,

and $ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial q^{j}} & = & \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x^{i}} \frac{\partial x^{i}}{\partial q^{j}} \end{eqnarray} $.

Mixed Partial Derivatives

The order of differentiation does not matter when the second partial derivatives exist and are continuous for all $(x,y)$ in the appropriate region.

By definition, the partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $x$ evaluated at the point $[x_{0},y_{0}]$ is

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} (x_{0},y_{0}) & = & \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \left[ \frac{f(x_{0} + h, y_{0}) - f(x_{0}, y_{0})}{h} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

Applying this definition again, but differentiating with respect to $y$ gives

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y \partial x} (x_{0},y_{0}) & = & \lim_{k \rightarrow 0} \left[ \frac{\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x_{0}, y_{0} + k) - \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x_{0}, y_{0})}{k} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

Expanding this out by substituting $\partial f / \partial x$ evaluated at the point required gives

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y \partial x} (x_{0},y_{0}) & = & \lim_{k \rightarrow 0} \left[ \frac{ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \left[ \frac{f(x_{0} + h, y_{0} + k) - f(x_{0}, y_{0} + k)}{h} \right] - \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \left[ \frac{f(x_{0} + h, y_{0}) - f(x_{0}, y_{0})}{h} \right]}{k} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

By the theorems of limits we can combine the limit for $h$ as follows

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y \partial x} (x_{0},y_{0}) & = & \lim_{k \rightarrow 0} \left[ \frac{ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \left[ \frac{f(x_{0} + h, y_{0} + k) - f(x_{0}, y_{0} + k) - f(x_{0} + h, y_{0}) + f(x_{0}, y_{0})}{h} \right]}{k} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

Define

$ \begin{eqnarray} \phi(x, y) & = & f(x + h, y) - f(x, y) \end{eqnarray} $

Then

$ \begin{eqnarray} \phi(x_{0}, y_{0} + k) & = & f(x_{0} + h, y_{0} + k) - f(x_{0}, y_{0} + k) \end{eqnarray} $

And

$ \begin{eqnarray} \phi(x_{0}, y_{0}) & = & f(x_{0} + h, y_{0}) - f(x_{0}, y_{0}) \end{eqnarray} $

Subtracting

$ \begin{eqnarray} \phi(x_{0}, y_{0} + k) - \phi(x_{0}, y_{0}) & = & f(x_{0} + h, y_{0} + k) - f(x_{0}, y_{0} + k) - f(x_{0} + h, y_{0}) + f(x_{0}, y_{0}) \end{eqnarray} $

Apply the Mean Value Theorem to convert the differences into partial derivatives:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y \partial x} (x_{0},y_{0}) & = & \lim_{k \rightarrow 0} \left[ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \left[ \frac{ \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y} (x_{0}, y_{0} + \theta_{k} k)}{h} \right] \right] \end{eqnarray} $

where $0 ≤ \theta_{k} ≤ 1$.

We now convert the $\phi$ partial derivative back to a partial derivative of $f$, noting the $x$ displacement by $h$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y \partial x} (x_{0},y_{0}) & = & \lim_{k \rightarrow 0} \left[ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \left[ \frac{ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} (x_{0} + h, y_{0} + \theta_{k} k) - \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} (x_{0}, y_{0} + \theta_{k} k)}{h} \right] \right] \end{eqnarray} $

Apply the Mean Value Theorem a second time gives

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial y \partial x} (x_{0},y_{0}) & = & \lim_{k \rightarrow 0} \left[ \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \left[\frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x \partial y} (x_{0} + \theta_{h} h, y_{0} + \theta_{k} k) \right] \right] \end{eqnarray} $

where $0 ≤ \theta_{h} ≤ 1$.

Finally, since the mixed partial derivatives have been assumed to be continuous at $[x_{0},y_{0}]$, the limit on the right hand side can be replaced by the value of the mixed partial derivative evaluated at $[x_{0},y_{0}]$.
