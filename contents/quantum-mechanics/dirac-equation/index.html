Dirac Equation

Notation

$ \begin{eqnarray} x^{\mu} & = & (x^0, x^1, x^2, x^3) = (ct, x, y, z) \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} x_{\mu} & = & (x_0, x_1, x_2, x_3) = (ct, -x, -y, -z) \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \partial_{\mu} & = & \frac{\partial}{\partial x^{\mu}} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Box & = & \gamma^{\mu} \partial_{\mu} \end{eqnarray} $

Generalizations about traditional Quantum Dynamical Equations

The wavefunction is typically a vector in Hilbert space.

Real wavefunctions correspond to electrically neutral particles.

Complex wavefunctions correspond to charged particles.

Multiple component wavefunctions correspond to spin.

Linear momentum operators are the generators of translations.

Angular momentum operators are the generators of rotations.

The meaning of the quantum dynamical equation varies depending upon the particle and any approximation.

Rotation Group $O(3)$

A general spatial rotation using real matrices transforms column vectors according to

$ \begin{eqnarray} x' & = & R x. \end{eqnarray} $

The rotation preserves the length of the column vector and so for an arbitrary vector $x$,

$ \begin{eqnarray} x^{T} x & = & (x')^{T} x' \\ & = & (R x)^{T} R x \\ & = & x^T (R^T R) x. \end{eqnarray} $

Since $x$ is arbitrary we obtain the condition $R^{T} R = I$.

By considering successive rotations, the identity element, and the inverse $R^T$ of an element $R$, we see that rotation matrices form a group called the rotation group:

$ \begin{eqnarray} O(n) & = & \{ R \in \mathrm{Mat}(n, \mathbb{R}) | R^{T} R = I \}. \end{eqnarray} $

For example, consider the passive rotation (rotate the axes, leave objects fixed) about the $z$-axis:

$ \begin{eqnarray} R_{z}(\psi) & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccccc} \cos \psi & \sin \psi & 0 \\ - \sin \psi & \cos \psi & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] \end{eqnarray} $.

(Prove this by using elementary trigonometry.) Now use cyclic permutations to show:

$ \begin{eqnarray} R_{x}(\varphi) & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos \varphi & \sin \varphi \\ 0 & - \sin \varphi & \cos \varphi \\ \end{array} \right] \end{eqnarray} $,

and

$ \begin{eqnarray} R_{y}(\theta) & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccccc} \cos \theta & 0 & - \sin \theta \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ \sin \theta & 0 & \cos \theta \\ \end{array} \right] \end{eqnarray} $.

We now proceed to compute the matrix generators of a rotation by examining the behavior close to the identity transformation. If $J_x$ is the generator (matrix) for the finite rotation $R_x(\varphi)$ then

$ \begin{eqnarray} R_x(\varphi) & = & \exp(i J_x \varphi) \\ & = & \lim_{N \rightarrow \infty} \left(I + i J_x \frac{\varphi}{N} \right)^{N}. \end{eqnarray} $

Combining this definition and the matrix $R_x(\varphi)$ enables us to derive $J_x$:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \lim_{\Delta \varphi \rightarrow 0} R_{x}(\Delta \varphi) & = & I + \lim_{\Delta \varphi \rightarrow 0} i J_x \Delta \varphi \\ & = & \lim_{\Delta \varphi \rightarrow 0} \left[ \begin{array}{cccccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos \Delta \varphi & \sin \Delta \varphi \\ 0 & - \sin \Delta \varphi & \cos \Delta \varphi \\ \end{array} \right] \\ & = & \lim_{\Delta \varphi \rightarrow 0} \left[ \begin{array}{cccccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & \Delta \varphi \\ 0 & - \Delta \varphi & 1 \\ \end{array} \right] \\ & = & I + \lim_{\Delta \varphi \rightarrow 0} \Delta \varphi \left[ \begin{array}{cccccc} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \\ \end{array} \right] \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Hence

$ \begin{eqnarray} J_{x} & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccccc} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -i \\ 0 & i & 0 \\ \end{array} \right], J_{y} & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccccc} 0 & 0 & i \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -i & 0 & 0 \\ \end{array} \right], J_{z} & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccccc} 0 & -i & 0 \\ i & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \end{array} \right]. \end{eqnarray} $

A (general) rotation about an axis $\hat{n}$ by an angle $\theta$ is given by

$ \begin{equation*} R_{\hat{n}}(\theta) = \exp i (J \cdot \hat{n}) \theta \end{equation*} $.

Every rotation matrix in $O(3)$ is determined by 3 parameters.

$SU(2)$ and spinors

Consider the set of unitary $n \times n$ matrices with determinant $+1$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} SU(n) & = & \{U \in \mathrm{Mat}(n, \Complex) | U U^{\dagger} = 1, det(U) = 1 \} \end{eqnarray} $

We will examine what this implies in the specific case of $SU(2)$. Let

$ \begin{eqnarray} U & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} a & b \\ c & d \end{array} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

Since the determinant of $U$ is unity and $\Inverse{U} = \HermitianConjugate{U}$,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{array} \right] & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} \ComplexConjugate{a} & \ComplexConjugate{c} \\ \ComplexConjugate{b} & \ComplexConjugate{d} \end{array} \right]. \end{eqnarray} $

and by comparing matrix elements we find $c = - \ComplexConjugate{b}$ and $d = \ComplexConjugate{a}$. This enables us to re-write $U$ and the determinant condition as

$ \begin{eqnarray} U & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} a & b \\ - \ComplexConjugate{b} & \ComplexConjugate{a} \end{array} \right], \NormSquared{a} + \NormSquared{b} = 1. \end{eqnarray} $

The previous equation implies that $U$ is determined by 3 real parameters.

Consider now the transformation of a two component complex column vector, which we call a spinor.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Ket{\psi} & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} \psi_{1} \\ \psi_{2} \end{array} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Ket{\psi} & \mapsto & U \Ket{\psi} & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} a & b \\ - \ComplexConjugate{b} & \ComplexConjugate{a} \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} \psi_{1} \\ \psi_{2} \end{array} \right] & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} a \psi_{1} + b \psi_{2} \\ - \ComplexConjugate{b} \psi_{1} + \ComplexConjugate{a} \psi_{2} \end{array} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Bra{\psi} & = & \HermitianConjugate{\Ket{\psi}} & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} \ComplexConjugate{\psi_{1}} & \ComplexConjugate{\psi_{2}} \end{array} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

The quantity

$ \begin{eqnarray} \BraKet{\psi}{\psi} & = & \NormSquared{\psi_1} + \NormSquared{\psi_2} \label{eq:scalarProduct} \end{eqnarray} $

is invariant under the unitary transormation $\eqref{eq:unitaryTransformation}$.

We see that $\Ket{\psi}$ and $\Bra{\psi}$ (the Hermitian conjugate) transform in different ways:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Bra{\psi} = \HermitianConjugate{\Ket{\psi}} \mapsto \HermitianConjugate{(U \Ket{\psi})} = \HermitianConjugate{\Ket{\psi}} \HermitianConjugate{U} = \Bra{\psi} \HermitianConjugate{U} \label{eq:unitaryTransformation} \end{eqnarray} $
