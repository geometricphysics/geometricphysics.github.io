Bohmian Mechanics

Overview

Bohmian mechanics is a non-relativistic (Galilei-invariant) quantum theory about the motion of point particles.

Bohmian mechanics aims to be observer-independent by replacing the measurement postulate of orthodox quantum theory with a postulate connecting the velocity of each particle to the wavefunction. The phase function, $S$, in the complex polar form of the wavefunction,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \psi & = & | \psi | \exp(\frac{i}{\hbar} S) \end{eqnarray} $,

acts to 'guide' the $k$-th particle according to the relationship

$ \begin{eqnarray} v_{k} & \propto & \nabla_{k} S \end{eqnarray} $.

Notation

In quantum mechanics, the symbol $i$ is widely used for the complex imaginary number. We will use $k$ as the particle index. $J$ will be used for probablity current densities.

Kinematics

The law of motion is a first order differential equation and thus Bohmian mechanics is naturally formulated on configuration space.

The configuration of a system of $N$ particles is the vector

$ \begin{eqnarray} q & = & (q_1, \ldots , q_N) \in \mathbb{R}^{3N} \end{eqnarray} $

, where $q_k \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is the position of the $k$-th particle.

For given initial data the theory yields a trajectory

$ \begin{eqnarray} t \mapsto q(t) \in \mathbb{R}^{3N} \end{eqnarray} $

in configuration space, i.e. the positions of all particles at all times.

The positions are the only output of the theory. Everything else (velocities, momenta, energies, spin etc.) must be deduced from the positions.

Dynamics

Postulate 1: The Schrödinger equation

The wavefunction, $\psi(q,t) : \mathbb{R}^{3N} \times \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{C}$, satisfies the Schrödinger equation continuously at all times.

$ \begin{eqnarray} i \hbar \partial_t \psi & = & \sum_{k = 1}^{N} - \frac{\hbar^2}{2 m_k} \nabla_k^2 \psi + V \psi \end{eqnarray} $

Postulate 2: The de Broglie guiding equation

The position of the $k$-th particle is related to the probability current density $J_{k}$ by the relation

$ \begin{eqnarray} v_k(q,t) & = & \frac{J_{k}(q,t)}{\rho(q,t)} = \frac{J_{k}(q,t)}{| \psi |^2} \end{eqnarray} $

Notice that this is the standard formula connecting current, density and velocity except that the density is determined by the global wavefunction, and not a partial density.

The velocity of the $k$-th particle is determined by the equation of motion

$ \begin{eqnarray} v_k & = & \frac{d q_k}{d t} = \frac{\hbar}{m_k} ? \left(\frac{\nabla_k \psi}{\psi} \right) \end{eqnarray} $

In other words, the theory really enables us to compute velocities which we can then integrate to determine the positions.

Pilot Wave Concept

The pilot wave idea is that the velocity, $v_k$, of the $k$-th particle is guided by the global wavefunction, $\psi$, which we can write in complex polar form as

$ \begin{eqnarray} \psi(q,t) & = & | \psi | \exp(\frac{i}{\hbar} S(q,t)) \end{eqnarray} $

, where the phase function $S(q,t) : \mathbb{R^{3N} \times \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}}$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} v_k \propto \nabla_k S \end{eqnarray} $

Continuity Equation

We first derive the continuity equation in the standard way for the Schrödinger equation:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \partial_t \rho & = & \partial_t |\psi|^2 \\ & = & \partial_t (\psi \psi^{*}) \\ & = & (\partial_{t} \psi) \psi^{*} + \psi (\partial_t \psi^{*}) \\ & = & \left(\sum_{k = 1}^{N} \frac{i \hbar}{2 m_k} \nabla_k^2 \psi - \frac{i V}{\hbar} \psi \right) \psi^{*} + \psi \left(\sum_{k = 1}^{N} - \frac{i \hbar}{2 m_k} \nabla_k^2 \psi^* + \frac{i V^*}{\hbar} \psi^* \right) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Assuming that the potential is real ($V : \mathbb{R}$), then $V = V^*$, and the terms involving the potential cancel.

Additionally, if we define the probablility current for the $k$-th particle as

$ \begin{eqnarray} J_k & = & \frac{i \hbar}{2 m_k} (\psi \nabla_k \psi^* - \psi^* \nabla_k \psi) \end{eqnarray} $,

and then use the identity $\nabla(f \vec{A}) = (\nabla f) \cdot \vec{A} + f (\nabla \cdot \vec{A})$, we find

$ \begin{eqnarray} \nabla_k \cdot J_k & = & \frac{i \hbar}{2 m_k} \nabla_k \cdot (\psi \nabla_k \psi^* - \psi^* \nabla_k \psi) \\ & = & \frac{i \hbar}{2 m_k} \left(\nabla_k \psi \cdot \nabla_k \psi^* + \psi \nabla_k^2 \psi^* - \nabla_k \psi^* \cdot \nabla_k \psi - \psi^* \nabla_k^2 \psi \right) \\ & = & \frac{i \hbar}{2 m_k} \left(\psi \nabla_k^2 \psi^* - \psi^* \nabla_k^2 \psi \right). \end{eqnarray} $

Combining this expression with $\partial_t \rho$ we obtain the continuity equation

$ \begin{eqnarray} \partial_t \rho + \sum_{k=1}^N \nabla_k \cdot J_k = 0. \end{eqnarray} $

We now use the complex polar form of the wavefunction to obtain the continuity equation in terms of $\rho$ and $S$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} J_k & = & \frac{i \hbar}{2 m_k} (\psi \nabla_k \psi^* - \psi^* \nabla_k \psi) \\ & = & \frac{i \hbar |\psi|}{2 m_k} \left( \exp(\frac{iS}{\hbar}) \nabla_k (|\psi|\exp(-\frac{iS}{\hbar})) - \exp(-\frac{iS}{\hbar}) \nabla_k (|\psi|\exp(\frac{iS}{\hbar})) \right) \\ & = & \frac{i \hbar |\psi|}{2 m_k} \left(\nabla_k |\psi| - |\psi| \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k S - \nabla_k |\psi| - |\psi| \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k S \right) \\ & = & |\psi|^2 \frac{\nabla_k S}{m_k} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The continuity equation for $|\psi|^2 : \mathbb{R}^{3N} \times \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ becomes

$ \begin{eqnarray} \partial_t |\psi|^2 + \sum_{k=1}^N \nabla_k \cdot \left(|\psi|^2 \frac{\nabla_k S}{m_k} \right) = 0. \end{eqnarray} $

The $k$-th particle velocity

Equipped with the quantum probability current $J_k$ and the guiding equation postulate, we deduce that the particle velocity is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} v_k & = & \frac{J_k}{|\psi|^2} = \frac{\nabla_k S}{m_k} \end{eqnarray} $

The wave function generates a time-dependent vector field $v_k(q,t)$ on configuration space. Its integral curves are the possible trajectories of Bohmian particles.

Given initial data $q(0)$ and $\psi(q,0)$, $q(t)$ is determined for all times.

The quantum Hamilton-Jacobi for $N$ particles

We now seek the Schrödinger equation in terms of the complex polar variables $|\psi|$ and $S$.

We first evaluate $\nabla_k^2 \psi$:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \nabla_k^2 \psi & = & \nabla_k \cdot \left[ \nabla_k ( |\psi| \exp(\frac{i}{\hbar}S) ) \right] \\ & = & \nabla_k \cdot \left[ \exp(\frac{i}{\hbar}S) \left( \nabla_k |\psi| + |\psi| \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k S \right) \right] \\ & = & \exp(\frac{i}{\hbar}S) \left( \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k S \cdot \left[\nabla_k |\psi| + |\psi| \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k S \right] + \nabla_k \cdot \left[\nabla_k |\psi| + |\psi| \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k S \right] \right) \\ & = & \exp(\frac{i}{\hbar}S) \left( \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k S \cdot \nabla_k |\psi| - |\psi| \frac{1}{\hbar^2} |\nabla_k S|^2 + \nabla_k^2 |\psi| + \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k |\psi| \cdot \nabla_k S + |\psi| \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k^2 S \right) \\ & = & \exp(\frac{i}{\hbar}S) \left( \nabla_k^2 |\psi| - |\psi| \frac{1}{\hbar^2} |\nabla_k S|^2 + \frac{2i}{\hbar} \nabla_k |\psi| \cdot \nabla_k S + |\psi| \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k^2 S \right) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Now we work on $\partial_t \psi$:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \partial_t \psi & = & \partial_t \left(|\psi| \exp(\frac{i}{\hbar}S) \right) \\ & = & \exp(\frac{i}{\hbar}S) \left( \partial_t |\psi| + |\psi| \frac{i}{\hbar} \partial_t S \right) \end{eqnarray} $

Substituting these two expressions into the Schrödinger equation, and after cancelling the $\exp(\frac{i}{\hbar}S)$ we obtain

$ \begin{eqnarray} i \hbar \partial_t |\psi| - |\psi| \partial_t S & = & \sum_{k=1}^N - \frac{\hbar^2}{2 m_k} \left( \nabla_k^2 |\psi| - |\psi| \frac{1}{\hbar^2} |\nabla_k S|^2 + \frac{2i}{\hbar} \nabla_k |\psi| \cdot \nabla_k S + |\psi| \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k^2 S \right) + V |\psi| \end{eqnarray} $

We now expand the continuity equation to obtain an expression for $\partial_t |\psi|$:

$ \begin{eqnarray} 2 |\psi| \partial_t |\psi| & = & - \sum_{k=1}^N \left(2 |\psi| \nabla_k |\psi| \cdot \frac{\nabla_k S}{m_k} + |\psi|^2 \frac{\nabla_k^2 S}{m_k} \right) \end{eqnarray} $

We now expand the continuity equation to obtain an expression for $i \hbar \partial_t |\psi|$:

$ \begin{eqnarray} i \hbar \partial_t |\psi| & = & \sum_{k=1}^N - \frac{\hbar^2}{2 m_k} \left(\frac{2i}{\hbar} \nabla_k |\psi| \cdot \nabla_k S + |\psi| \frac{i}{\hbar} \nabla_k^2 S \right) \end{eqnarray} $

And by an amazing stroke of good fortune our Schrödinger equation simplifies to the quantum Hamilton-Jacobi:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \partial_t S + \sum_{k=1}^N \frac{|\nabla_k S|^2}{2 m_k} + V + \sum_{k=1}^N - \frac{\hbar^2}{2 m_k} \left(\frac{\nabla_k^2 |\psi|}{|\psi|} \right) = 0 \end{eqnarray} $

The second term is the total classical kinetic energy $T$

$ \begin{eqnarray} T & = & \sum_{k=1}^N \frac{|\nabla_k S|^2}{2 m_k} \end{eqnarray} $

The final term is quantum mechanical in origin and represents a quantum potential.
