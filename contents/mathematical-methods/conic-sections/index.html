Conic Sections

Overview

Our goal will be to provide a uniform treatment of conic sections in terms of the Directrix and Focus.

We will show that the loci are indeed sections of a cone.

We then show how the character of the orbit depends on the eccentricity.

We will replace the usual disjointed definitions of the parabola, ellipse and hyperbola with theorems.

We will take into consideration the circle as well as the intersection of two lines.

The classification into elliptic, parabolic and hyperbolic occurs in other areas of mathematics.

Geometry of Conic Sections

A conic section is the locus of a point whose distance from a given point, the focus, $F$, is proportional to its perpendicular distance from a given line not containing $F$, the directrix, $L$. The constant of proportionality is called the eccentricity, $e$.

Definitions

The linear eccentricity, $c$ is the distance from a focus to center.

The latus rectum, $2l$ is chord parallel to the directrix and passing through the focus.

The semi-latus rectum, $l$ is half the latus rectum.

The focal parameter, $p$ is the distance from the focus to the directrix.

For an ellipse, the semi-major axis, $a$ is half the length of the major axis.

For an ellipse, the semi-minor axis, $b$ is half the length of the minor axis.

Algebra of Conic Sections

In the diagram below, the conic is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Norm{FP} & = & e \Norm{DP} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} r & = & e (p + r \cos \theta) \end{eqnarray} $

Geometry of conic sections

Solving for $r$,

$ \begin{eqnarray} r & = & \frac{e p}{1 - e \cos \theta} \end{eqnarray} $

When $\theta = \pi /2$, the radius is equal to the semi-latus rectum (half-side straight).

$ \begin{eqnarray} l & = & e p \end{eqnarray} $

i.e., the semi-latus rectum is the eccentricity times the focal parameter.

In applications involving orbits we often introduce the reciprocal of $r$, $u$ so that

$ \begin{eqnarray} u & = & \frac{1}{r} = \frac{1}{l} (1 - e \cos \theta) \end{eqnarray} $

Put the origin at the focus and use cartesian coordinates

$ \begin{eqnarray} x & = & r \cos \theta \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} y & = & r \sin \theta \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} r & = & (x^2+y^2)^\frac{1}{2} \end{eqnarray} $

The equation for the orbit becomes

$ \begin{eqnarray} (1-e^2)x^2 + y^2 - 2 e l x - l^2 & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

Parabola

When $e = 1$, the point $P$ is equidistant from the focus $F$ and the directrix $L$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} y^2 & = & 2 p x + p^2 \end{eqnarray} $

Ellipse

When $e \lt 1$, $1 / r$ never becomes zero, and so $r$ remains finite.

Comparing the equation for the orbit with the equation of an ellipse (where the parameters $a,b,c$ are defined already)

$ \begin{eqnarray} \left( \frac{x-c}{a} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{y}{b} \right)^2 & = & 1 \end{eqnarray} $

We find for the linear eccentricity, $c$

$ \begin{eqnarray} c & = & \frac{le}{1-e^2} \end{eqnarray} $

We find for the minor semi-axis, $b$

$ \begin{eqnarray} b & = & \frac{l}{(1-e^2)^{\frac{1}{2}}} \end{eqnarray} $

We find for the major semi-axis, $a$

$ \begin{eqnarray} a & = & \frac{l}{1-e^2} \end{eqnarray} $

We have also proved that

$ \begin{eqnarray} ae & = & c \end{eqnarray} $

With a little algebra we can also show

$ \begin{eqnarray} c & = & \sqrt{a^2 - b^2} \end{eqnarray} $

Hence

$ \begin{eqnarray} e & = & \sqrt{1 - (b/a)^2} \end{eqnarray} $

and

$ \begin{eqnarray} l & = & \frac{b^2}{a} \end{eqnarray} $

Hyperbola

When $e \gt 1$, $1 / r$ is zero for two values of $\theta$.

For the Hyperbola we write

$ \begin{eqnarray} \left( \frac{x+c}{a} \right)^2 - \left( \frac{y}{b} \right)^2 & = & 1 \end{eqnarray} $

We find for the linear eccentricity, $c$

$ \begin{eqnarray} c & = & \frac{le}{e^2 - 1} \end{eqnarray} $

We find for the minor semi-axis, $b$

$ \begin{eqnarray} b & = & \frac{l}{(e^2 - 1)^{\frac{1}{2}}} \end{eqnarray} $

We find for the major semi-axis, $a$

$ \begin{eqnarray} a & = & \frac{l}{e^2 - 1} \end{eqnarray} $

We have also proved that

$ \begin{eqnarray} ae & = & c \end{eqnarray} $

With a little algebra we can also show

$ \begin{eqnarray} c & = & \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \end{eqnarray} $

Hence

$ \begin{eqnarray} e & = & \sqrt{1 + (b/a)^2} \end{eqnarray} $

and

$ \begin{eqnarray} l & = & \frac{b^2}{a} \end{eqnarray} $
