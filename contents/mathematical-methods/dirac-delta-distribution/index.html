Dirac delta distribution

Definition

The Dirac delta distribution is defined by the following properties:

Spike

$ \begin{eqnarray} \delta(x) = 0, x \neq 0 \end{eqnarray} $,

Normalized

$ \begin{eqnarray} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dx \delta(x) = 1 \end{eqnarray} $,

Selection

$ \begin{eqnarray} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dx f(x) \delta(x) = f(0) \end{eqnarray} $

Approximation

The Dirac delta distribution may be approximated by a variety of functions:

Rectangle

$ \delta_n(x) = \left\{ \begin{eqnarray} & n &, -1/2n \lt x \lt 1/2n \\ & 0 & , otherwise \end{eqnarray} \right. $

The integral properties follow easily from the definition.

Gaussian

$ \delta_n(x) = \begin{eqnarray} \frac{n}{\sqrt{\pi}} exp(-n^2 x^2) \end{eqnarray} $

Verifying this approximation involves the standard approach of converting the line integral into a two-dimensional integral over the plane and converting to polar coordinates.

This form of the distribution is useful because it is easy to differentiate.

Tangent

$ \delta_n(x) = \begin{eqnarray} \frac{n}{\pi} \frac{1}{1 + n^2 x^2} \end{eqnarray} $

Verify this approximation using the substitution $n x = \tan(\theta$)

This form is also easy to differentiate.

Fourier

$ \delta_n(x) = \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\sin(n x)}{\pi x} = \frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-n}^{n} dk \exp(-i k x) \end{eqnarray} $

Proving the normalization condition requires evaluating the sine integral. This can be done through contour integration or by an involved elementary method.
