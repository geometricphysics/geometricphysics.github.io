Common Groups

General Linear Group $GL(n, \Field{F})$

$ \begin{eqnarray} GL(n,\Field{F}) & = & \{M \in \mathrm{Mat}(n, \Field{F}) | det(M) \neq 0 \} \end{eqnarray} $

$GL(n,\Field{F})$ is a $(n^2 \times \OrderOfGroup{\Field{F}})$-parameter Lie group.

Orthogonal Group $O(n)$

$ \begin{eqnarray} O(n) & = & \{M \in \mathrm{Mat}(n, \RealField) | \Transpose{M} M = M \Transpose{M} = I \} \end{eqnarray} $

The statement $\Transpose{M} M = I$ means that the column vectors of $M$ are mutually orthonormal.

The statement $M \Transpose{M} = I$ means that the row vectors of $M$ are mutually orthonormal.

The orthonormal condition is a necessary and sufficient condition for the transformation to preserve the length of a vector in the appropriate space:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Ket{\Primed{x}} & = & M \Ket{x} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \BraKet{\Primed{x}}{\Primed{x}} & = & \Bra{x} \Transpose{M} M \Ket{x} = \BraKet{x}{x} \end{eqnarray} $

The orthonormal condition also implies that $det(M) = \pm 1$. This makes the orthogonal group a subgroup of the general linear group.

For the field $\RealField$, the number of parameters of $GL(n,\RealField)$ is $n^2$. The orthogonality condition reduces the number of free parameters.

Special Orthogonal Group $SO(n)$

$ \begin{eqnarray} SO(n) & = & \{M \in \mathrm{Mat}(n, \RealField) | M \Transpose{M} = \Transpose{M} M = I, det(M) = 1 \} \end{eqnarray} $

Rotation group $SO(2)$

$ \begin{eqnarray} SO(2) & = & \{R \in \mathrm{Mat}(2, \mathbb{R}) | R^T R = 1, det(R) = 1 \} \end{eqnarray} $

For an active rotation (of the points in the coordinate frame counter-clockwise)

$ \begin{eqnarray} R(\phi) & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} \cos \phi & - \sin \phi \\ \sin \phi & \cos \phi \end{array} \right] & \in & SO(2), \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} x \\ y \end{array} \right] & \mapsto & R(\phi) \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} x \\ y \end{array} \right]. \end{eqnarray} $

For an infinitesimal rotation,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} x + dx \\ y + dy \end{array} \right] & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} 1 & - d \phi \\ d \phi & 1 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} x \\ y \end{array} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} dx \\ dy \end{array} \right] & = & d \phi J \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} x \\ y \end{array} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

where

$ \begin{eqnarray} J & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} 0 & - 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{array} \right] & = & \frac{d R}{d \phi} (0) \end{eqnarray} $

is called the infinitesimal generator of the rotation matrix.

Using Taylor's theorem to expand $R(\phi)$ about $R(0)$ we find

$ \begin{eqnarray} R(\phi) & = & \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{\phi^n}{n!} \frac{d^n R}{d \phi^n} (0) \\ & = & \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(\phi J)^n}{n!} \\ & = & \exp(\phi J) \\ & = & I \cos(\phi) + J \sin(\phi) \end{eqnarray} $

If we now consider an arbitrary differentiable function $F(x,y)$ under an infinitesimal rotation

$ \begin{eqnarray} F(x,y) & \mapsto & F(x+dx,y+dy) \\ & = & F(x,y) + \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} dy \\ & = & F(x,y) + \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} (- d \phi y) + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} (d \phi x) \\ & = & F(x,y) + d \phi \left(x \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} - y \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} \right) \\ & = & F(x,y) + d \phi L F, \\ \end{eqnarray} $

where $L$ is the differential operator

$ \begin{eqnarray} L & = & x \frac{\partial}{\partial y} - y \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Changing the notation for coordinates from $(x,y)$ to $(x^1,x^2)$, this operator can be written more compactly as

$ \begin{eqnarray} L & = & x^1 \frac{\partial}{\partial x^2} - x^2 \frac{\partial}{\partial x^1} \\ & = & x^1 \partial_2 - x^2 \partial_1 \\ & = & \varepsilon^{ij} x^{i} \partial_{j} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Unitary group $U(1)$

$ \begin{eqnarray} U(1) & = & \{z \in \mathbb{C} | z \bar{z} = 1 \} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \cos \phi + i \sin \phi & \in & U(1) \end{eqnarray} $

$Spin(2)$

$ \begin{eqnarray} Spin(2) & = & \{s \in \mathit{Cl}_2 | s \bar{s} = 1 \} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \cos \frac{\phi}{2} + e_{12} \sin \frac{\phi}{2} & \in & Spin(2) \end{eqnarray} $
