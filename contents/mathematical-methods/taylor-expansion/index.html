Taylor's Expansion

Introduction

Suppose that we have a function $f(x)$ with derivatives of all orders in some neighborhood containing the point $x = a$, then, at any point in the neighborhood, the function can be represented by a series in powers of $x-a$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(x-a)^{n}}{n!} f^{(n)}(a) \end{eqnarray} $

Derivation

One approach is to assume that an infinite series expansion for $f(x)$ exists and then to compute the coefficients.

$ \begin{eqnarray} f(x) = a_{0} + a_{1} (x-a) + a_{2} (x-a)^{2} + \cdots + a_{r} (x-a)^{r} + \cdots \end{eqnarray} $

By differentiating, and setting $x=a$ each time, we can show

$ \begin{eqnarray} a_{r} = \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!} \end{eqnarray} $

However, this approach does not give us much insight into the error caused by truncating the series.
