k-Calculus

Introduction

We have seen how an observer, using only light signals and a single fixed clock, can assign spacetime coordinates to an event. We now investigate how the relative velocity of two observers affects the interval for received pulses compared to the transmission interval measured on the respective clocks. This will give rise to a $k$-function of the relative velocity.

$k$-Calculus is not actually calculus in the sense of infinitesimals and sums. We're using the more general meaning of the word as a procedure by which we calculate things. In particular we are talking about a procedure for transforming the coordinates of an event between observers in relative motion. What's special about k-Calculus is that it avoids the intermediate step of creating a coordinate lattice with associated synchronized clocks. By using the more fundamental technique of measuring the elapsed time of an out-and-back signal, we hope to make the underlying physical principles more obvious.

In addition, we will seek ways to make the invariants manifest and take a geometric approach that avoids special coordinate systems, again to make the underlying physics more obvious.

The name k-Calculus comes from a function $k(v)$ that emerges in the analysis when we make the assumption that only relative velocity matters when transforming coordinates between inertial frames. This is equivalent to the Relativity Principle.

The Relative Velocity

Define the relative velocity $\Vector{v}$ of the clocks to be positive when the clocks are receeding from each other.

The ratio of the received pulse interval (measured by the receiving clock) to transmitted pulse interval (measured by the sending clock) defines the function $k(v)$:

For example, considering the signals emitted at $t_1$ and $t_2$ and received by the moving clock:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Delta \tau = \tau_f - \tau_i & = & k(v) (t_2 - t_1). \end{eqnarray} $

Similarly,

$ \begin{eqnarray} t_4 - t_3 & = & k(v) (\tau_f - \tau_i) & = & k(v) \Delta \tau. \end{eqnarray} $

Let us calculate an expression for the velocity of the moving clock relative to the inertial frame.

$ \begin{eqnarray} v & = & \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} & = & \frac{x_f - x_i}{t_f - t_i}. \end{eqnarray} $

Recalling the expressions for the coordinates in terms of the times at which measuring signals are sent and received, we find

$ \begin{eqnarray} v & = & \frac{(t_4 - t_2) - (t_3 - t_1)}{(t_4 + t_2) - (t_3 + t_1)} & = & \frac{(t_4 - t_3) - (t_2 - t_1)}{(t_4 - t_3) + (t_2 - t_1)}. \end{eqnarray} $

Substituting the expressions for the differences gives,

$ \begin{eqnarray} v & = & \frac{k - (1/k)}{k + (1/k)} & = & \frac{k^2 - 1}{k^2 + 1}. \end{eqnarray} $

Solving for $k$,

$ \begin{eqnarray} v (k^2 + 1) & = & k^2 - 1, \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} 1 + v & = & k^2 (1 - v), \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} k(v) & = & \left( \frac{1 + v}{1 - v} \right)^{\frac{1}{2}}. \end{eqnarray} $

We remark the following two identities satisfied by $k$:

$ \begin{eqnarray} k(0) & = & 1, \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} k(\beta) k(- \beta) & = & 1. \end{eqnarray} $

Finally, we will encounter the quantity $\gamma(v) = (1 - v^2)^{- \frac{1}{2}}$. It will be useful to have an expression for $\gamma$ in terms of $k$. Since

$ \begin{eqnarray} v & = & \frac{k^2 - 1}{k^2 + 1}, \end{eqnarray} $ then

$ \begin{eqnarray} 1 + v & = & \frac{2k^2}{k^2 + 1}, \end{eqnarray} $ and

$ \begin{eqnarray} 1 - v & = & \frac{2}{k^2 + 1}. \end{eqnarray} $

It follows that

$ \begin{eqnarray} (1 + v)(1 - v) & = & (\frac{2k}{k^2 + 1})^2, \end{eqnarray} $ so

$ \begin{eqnarray} \gamma(v) & = & \frac{k^2 + 1}{2k}. \end{eqnarray} $

We also have

$ \begin{eqnarray} (1 + v) \gamma(v) & = & k, \end{eqnarray} $ and

$ \begin{eqnarray} (1 - v) \gamma(v) & = & \frac{1}{k}. \end{eqnarray} $
