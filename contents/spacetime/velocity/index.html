Velocity

Velocity Transformation

Using the Lorentz Transformation we can derive the transformation laws for the components of ordinary velocity.

$ \begin{eqnarray} u_x' & = & \frac{dx'}{dt'} & = & \frac{\gamma(v) \, (dx - v \, dt)}{\gamma(v) \, (dt - v \, dx)} & = & \frac{u_x - v}{1 - v \, u_x} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} u_y' & = & \frac{dy'}{dt'} & = & \frac{dy}{\gamma(v) \, (dt - v \, dx)} & = & \frac{u_y}{\gamma(v) \, (1 - v \, u_x)} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} u_z' & = & \frac{dz'}{dt'} & = & \frac{dz}{\gamma(v) \, (dt - v \, dx)} & = & \frac{u_z}{\gamma(v) \, (1 - v \, u_x)} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Notice that in the limit $v \rightarrow 0$ we recover the Galilean Transformation for velocities.

The inverse (velocity addition) transformations are found by interchanging primed and unprimed symbols and the replacement $v \rightarrow -v$:

$ \begin{eqnarray} u_x & = & \frac{u_x' + v}{1 + v \, u_x'} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} u_y & = & \frac{u_y'}{\gamma(v) \, (1 + v \, u_x')} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} u_z & = & \frac{u_z'}{\gamma(v) \, (1 + v \, u_x')} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Although we have derived a velocity transformation law, we haven't uncovered a four-velocity. An interesting way to derive a four-velocity, and provide an alternate route to the velocity transformation is by constructing a four velocity $U$ as follows:

$ \begin{eqnarray} U & = & \frac{d X}{d \tau}, \\ \end{eqnarray} $

where $d X$ is the four-displacement $(dt, d\Vector{x})$ and $d \tau = \sqrt{dt^2 - d\Vector{x}^2}$ is the proper time invariant interval.

$ \begin{eqnarray} U & = & \frac{(d t, d\Vector{x})}{\sqrt{dt^2 - d\Vector{x}^2}} = \gamma(u)(1, \Vector{u}). \\ \end{eqnarray} $

This can be used to recover the velocity transformation using the standard LT as follows.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \gamma(\Primed{u}) \Primed{u}_x & = & \gamma(v) (\gamma(u) u_x - v \gamma(u)) = \gamma(v) \gamma(u) (u_x - v) \\ \gamma(\Primed{u}) \Primed{u}_y & = & \gamma(u) u_y \\ \gamma(\Primed{u}) \Primed{u}_z & = & \gamma(u) u_z \\ \gamma(\Primed{u}) & = & \gamma(v) (\gamma(u) - v \gamma(u) u_x) = \gamma(v) \gamma(u) (1 - v \, u_x) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The transformation of the time component of $U$ provides exactly the factor to make the other components work out.

The four-velocity provides the most robust route to the four-momemntum, simply by multiplying $U$ by the rest mass, $m$.
