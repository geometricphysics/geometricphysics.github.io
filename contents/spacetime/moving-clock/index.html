Moving Clock

Path Dependence of Time

A moving clock, when compared to clocks along the path of the moving clock which are at rest in an inertial frame, will show a slower instantaneous rate.

Let $\tau$ be the time recorded by the moving clock and $(ct,x^i)$ an event along the world line of the moving clock.

If $v$ is the instantaneous speed and $\gamma(v) = (1 - (v/c)^2)^{\frac{1}{2}}$, then

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d \tau}{d t} & = & \frac{1}{\gamma(v)} \end{eqnarray} $

Notice that, consistent with the clock hypothesis, the rate of the moving clock depends only upon the instantaneous speed relative to the inertial frame, and not upon its acceleration.

Derivation of Moving Clock Rate

In the diagram below, the green points represent two events on the worldline of the clock moving through the inertial frame. This clock may be moving nonuniformly, but we only show a small segment of the path. At the initial event, $E_i$, the moving clock records the time $\tau_i$. At the final event, $E_f$, the moving clock records the time $\tau_f$. The increment of time recorded by the moving clock is then $\Delta \tau = \tau_f - \tau_i$.

The red points represent events on the worldline of a clock at rest with respect to the inertial system.

The blue lines represent light signals emitted at $t_1$ and $t_2$, reflected from the moving clock, and received at $t_3$ and $t_4$ respectively. The purpose of these light signals is to measure the time and position of the events $E_i$ and $E_f$ in the coordinates of the inertial frame, which is necessary in order to determine the average velocity of the moving clock. As the increment $\Delta t = t_f - t_i$ is made smaller and smaller, the average velocity becomes a better approximation to the instantaneous velocity. The slider may be used to vary the instantaneous velocity of the moving clock in the interval $(-1,1)$.

We want to calculate

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d \tau}{d t} & = & \lim_{\Delta t \rightarrow 0} \frac{\Delta \tau}{\Delta t} \\ & = & \lim_{\Delta t \rightarrow 0} \frac{\Delta \tau}{t_f - t_i} \end{eqnarray} $

We assume that the light takes equal time for the outbound trip as for the return trip:

$ \begin{eqnarray} t_i & = & \frac{1}{2}(t_3 - t_1), \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} t_f & = & \frac{1}{2}(t_4 - t_2). \end{eqnarray} $

Substituting $t_i$ and $t_f$ we find

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\Delta \tau}{\Delta t} & = & \frac{2 \Delta \tau}{(t_4 - t_3) - (t_2 - t_1)}. \end{eqnarray} $

Define

$ \begin{eqnarray} k(v) & = & \frac{\Delta T_{receive}}{\Delta T_{transmit}}, \end{eqnarray} $

where $\Delta T_{transmit}$ is the interval between transmitted pulses as measured by the transmitting clock, and $\Delta T_{receive}$ is the interval between received pulses as measured by the receiving clock. $v$ is the relative velocity and is positive when the transmitter and receiver are receeding from each other, negative when they are approaching each other, and zero when at relative rest.

For the signals transmitted at $t_1$ and $t_2$ and received by the moving clock:

$ \begin{eqnarray} k(v) & = & \frac{\Delta \tau}{t_2 - t_1}. \end{eqnarray} $

For the signals reflected by the moving clock and received at $t_3$ and $t_4$ by the clock at rest:

$ \begin{eqnarray} k(v) & = & \frac{t_4 - t_3}{\Delta \tau}. \end{eqnarray} $

Substituting $t_1$, $t_2$, $t_3$ and $t_4$ we now have

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\Delta \tau}{\Delta t} & = & \frac{2 k(v)}{k(v)^2 + 1}. \end{eqnarray} $

We now need to find the form for $k(v)$, which we do by first computing the moving clock velocity

$ \begin{eqnarray} \beta & = & \frac{v}{c} \\ & = & \frac{\Delta x}{c \Delta t} \\ & = & \frac{x_f - x_i}{c \Delta t} \\ & = & \frac{c(t_f - t_2) - c(t_i - t_1)}{c \Delta t} \\ & = & \frac{(t_f - t_i) - (t_2 - t_1)}{\Delta t} \\ & = & \frac{\Delta t - (\Delta \tau / k(v))}{\Delta t} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Rearranging this last expression we get

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\Delta \tau}{\Delta t} & = & (1 - \beta) k(v). \end{eqnarray} $

Equating the two expressions for $\Delta \tau / \Delta t$ and solving for $\beta$ we find the following identities:

$ \begin{eqnarray} 1 - \beta & = & \frac{2}{k^2 + 1} \end{eqnarray} $,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \beta & = & \frac{k^2 - 1}{k^2 + 1} \end{eqnarray} $, and

$ \begin{eqnarray} 1 + \beta & = & \frac{2 k^2}{k^2 + 1} \end{eqnarray} $.

Multiplying the two expressions for $\Delta \tau / \Delta t$ and writing the result in terms of $\beta$ we obtain

$ \begin{eqnarray} (\frac{\Delta \tau}{\Delta t})^2 & = & (1 + \beta)(1 - \beta) \end{eqnarray} $.
