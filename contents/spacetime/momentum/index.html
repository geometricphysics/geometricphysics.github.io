Momentum and Mass

Mass

We will use a very restricted argument to deduce the dependence of the inertial mass on the relative velocity. Using the result as a conjecture, we proceed to find a more general derivation.

Consider a glancing collision between a sphere at rest in Alice's frame and a sphere at rest in Bob's frame.

We make the collision glancing so that the transverse velocity imparted to each sphere is very small compared to the relative velocity $v$ of the two inertial frames.

By symmetry, the spheres will have equal and opposite transverse velocities in their respective frames of magnitude $u_{\perp}$.

Suppose that the mass of the moving sphere, $m(v)$ is a function of it's rest value $m$ as a result of the relative speed of the two frames. By conservation of the transverse momentum we have

$ \begin{eqnarray} m u_{\perp} & = & \frac{m(v) u_{\perp}}{\gamma(v)(1 \pm v \, u_{\perp})}, \end{eqnarray} $ where the choice of sign depends whether the collision is being examined from Alice or Bob's frame.

Considering the limit of an increasingly glancing blow so that $v \, u_{\perp} \ll 1$, we obtain

$ \begin{eqnarray} m(v) & = & \gamma(v) m \end{eqnarray} $

Momentum

We can derive an expression for the four-momentum using the four-velocity:

$ \begin{eqnarray} P & = & m \, U = \gamma(u) (m, m \Vector{u}) = (E, \Vector{p}) \end{eqnarray} $

This construction of another four-vector requires only that $m$ is a scalar.

This can be used to recover the Energy-momentum transformation using the standard LT as follows.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Primed{p}_x & = & \gamma(v) (p_x - v \, \gamma(u) \, m) \\ \Primed{p}_y & = & p_y \\ \Primed{p}_z & = & p_z \\ \gamma(\Primed{u}) \Primed{m} & = & \gamma(v) (\gamma(u) m - v \, p_x) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Energy Conservation

Consider a collision between two particles. Momentum conservation gives us that

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{p}_1 + \Vector{p}_2 & = & \Vector{p}_3 + \Vector{p}_4 \end{eqnarray} $

Construct the four-vector

$ \begin{eqnarray} P_1 + P_2 - P_3 - P_4 & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

If momentum is conserved in all frames then this four-vector must be the zero vector. This is the zero component theorem which can be proved by knowing that rotations mix space components and boosts mix space and time.

It follows that we must have energy conservation (the time components) with $E = \gamma(u) \, m \, c^2$.

We also have the scalar invariant $E^2 - (\Vector{p} c)^2 = m^2 c^4$.

Energy and Velocity from 4-Momentum

Consider a particle of rest mass $m$ and 4-momentum $P = (E(\Vector{v}), \Vector{p}) = \gamma(\Vector{v})m(1,\Vector{v})$. The observer has a 4-velocity $U = \gamma(\Vector{u})(1, \Vector{u})$. Using the metric $(+1,-1,-1,-1)$, we find the following invariants

$ \begin{eqnarray} U \cdot U & = & 1 \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} P \cdot P & = & E^2 - (\Vector{p})^2 = m^2 \end{eqnarray} $ (by going to rest frame of particle)

$ \begin{eqnarray} U \cdot P & = & m \gamma(\Vector{u}) \gamma(\Vector{v}) (1 - \Vector{u} \cdot \Vector{v}) = E(\Vector{v}) \end{eqnarray} $ (by going to the observer frame) where $E(\Vector{v})$ is the energy of the particle as measured by the observer.

The magnitude of the particle momentum measured by the observer is

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Norm{\Vector{p}} & = & [E^2 - P \cdot P]^\frac{1}{2} = [(U \cdot P)^2 - P \cdot P]^\frac{1}{2} \end{eqnarray} $

The ordinary velocity is related to the ordinary momentum

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{p} & = & \gamma(\Vector{v}) m \Vector{v} = E(\Vector{v}) \Vector{v} \end{eqnarray} $

There exists a 4-velocity $V$ in which the components measured by the observer are $(0, \Vector{v})$

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{P}{P \cdot U} - U & = & (1, \Vector{v}) - (1, \Vector{0}) = (0, \Vector{v}) \end{eqnarray} $
