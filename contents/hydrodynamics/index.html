Density

$ \begin{eqnarray} density & = & \frac{mass}{volume} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Pressure

$ \begin{eqnarray} pressure & = & \frac{force}{area} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Force Density

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Delta F_{x} & = & P(x) A - P(x + \Delta x) A \\ \frac{\Delta F_{x}}{\Delta V} & = & - \frac{[P(x + \Delta x) - P(x)]}{\Delta x} \\ f_{x} & = & - \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} \\ \mathbf{f} & = & - \nabla P \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Hydrostatic Pressure

Consider a horizontal massless plate of width $w$ and length $l$ at depth $h$ in fluid. Assume that the density of the fluid, $\rho$, is uniform.

Let P(h) be the pressure at depth $h$. For the plate to be in equilibrium, we must have

$ \begin{eqnarray} P(h) w l & = & P(0) w l + \rho w l h g \\ \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} P(h) & = & P(0) + \rho g h \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Pascal's Principle

A change in pressure is transmitted undeminished to all parts of the fluid.

Proove this by considering pressure differences horizontally and vertically in a gravitational field.

An application of this principle is a Hydraulic Lift.

Consider two pistons operating on a closed vessel filled with fluid...

$ \begin{eqnarray} F_{1} + P_{0} A_{1} & = & P A_{1} \\ F_{2} + P_{0} A_{2} & = & P A_{2} \\ \Rightarrow F_{1} A_{2} & = & F_{2} A_{1} \\ \Rightarrow \frac{F_{1}}{F_{2}} & = & \frac{A_{2}}{A_{1}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Archimedes Principle

The magnitude of the buoyant force is equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by the object.

$ \begin{eqnarray} F_{buoy} & = & P(h) A \\ & = & \rho g h A \\ & = & \rho V_{displaced} g \\ & = & mass_{displaced} g \\ & = & weight_{displaced} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Flow Rate and the Continuity Equation

The flow rate, $f$ is the volume of fluid that passes a particular area per unit time.

If the velocity of the fluid is $\vec{v}$ over an an area $dA \hat{n}$ then the incremental flow is $ \begin{eqnarray} df & = & dA \vec{v} \cdot \hat{n} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Integrating over the surface gives $ \begin{eqnarray} f & = & \int_{S} \vec{v} \cdot \hat{n} dA \\ \end{eqnarray} $

For a plane surface geometry and assuming a uniform velocity $ \begin{eqnarray} f & = & v A cos(\theta) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

For an incompressible fluid with no sinks or sources we get the Continuity Equation $ \begin{eqnarray} A_{1} v_{1} & = & A_{2} v_{2} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Bernoulli's Equation

Derive Bernoulli's theorem using the work-energy theorem and conservation of mass. There are two sytems doing work. One is due to the pressures in the fluid, the other is due to gravity. The work done is equal to the change in kinetic energy. This approach is less confusing than thinking of the potential energy as increasing.

When done this way, the density $\rho$ appears as a divisor for $P$.

This equation is a statement of energy conservation and mass conservation within a fluid. In deriving Bernoulli's equation we make a number of assumptions:

  • The fluid is moving in a steady state
  • The fluid is incompressible
  • The fluid is frictionless
  • The only external force is gravitational

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{P}{\rho} + \frac{1}{2} v^{2} + g h & = & constant \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Venturi Effect

Pressure is lower where the flow speed is greater.

Torricelli's Theorem

This theorem concerns the exit speed of a fluid from a hole in a vessel which is a specified height below the surface. It is a consequence of Bernoulli's Theorem, that is, energy conservation.

$ \begin{eqnarray} v & = & \sqrt{2 g h} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Syphon

What is the maximum height that a syphon can lift a fluid?

$ \begin{eqnarray} h_{max} & = & \frac{P_{atm}}{\rho g} - s \\ \end{eqnarray} $

where

  • $h$ is the height of the syphon above the fluid surface
  • $P_{atm}$ is atmospheric pressure
  • $\rho$ is the density of the fluid
  • $s$ is the vertical drop from the fluid surface to the exit point of the syphon
