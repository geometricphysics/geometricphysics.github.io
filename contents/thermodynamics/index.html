Thermodynamics

Thermal Equilibrium

A condition in which two bodies in thermal contact have no net exchange of energy in the form of heat.

The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

If two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, then the two bodies are in thermal equilibrium with each other.

The First Law of Thermodynamics

This law is a statement of conservation of energy

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Delta U & = & Q + W \\ \end{eqnarray} $ where

  • $U$ is the internal energy of the system
  • $W = - P \Delta V$ is the work done on an ideal gas

The Second Law of Thermodynamics

For any cyclic heat engine, some exhaust heat is always produced.

$ \begin{eqnarray} e & = & \frac{Q_{H} - Q_{C}}{Q_{H}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $ where

  • $e$ is the thermal efficiency of the heat engine
  • $Q_{H}$ is the heat supplied to the engine in one cycle
  • $Q_{C}$ is the heat exhaust by the engine in one cycle

The Carnot Cycle

A heat engine utilizing the Carnot cycle has the highest possible efficiency consistent with the Second Law of Thermodynamics.

We use as our model for a heat engine, an ideal gas enclosed by a cylinder and piston. The heat engine moves between hot and cold resevoirs with temperatures $T_H$ and $T_C$ respectively. When in contact with a resevoir we perform an isothermal process. In between we perform adiabatic changes in order to match the temperatures.

Adiabatic Compression

The heat engine is thermally isolated. All the work done on the gas goes to increasing the thermal energy of the gas.

The thermal energy of the gas depends only on the temperature.

$ \begin{eqnarray} dE_{thermal} & = & N C_V dT \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} dW & = & -P dV \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} dQ & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} dE_{thermal} & = & dQ + dW \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} N C_V dT & = & -P dV \end{eqnarray} $

We first calculate the final volume by eliminating $P$ using the ideal gas law.

$ \begin{eqnarray} N C_V dT & = & - \left(\frac{N k_B T}{V} \right) dV \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{C_V}{k_B} \int_{T_i}^{T_f} \frac{dT}{T} & = & - \int_{V_i}^{V_f} \frac{dV}{V} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{C_V}{k_B} \ln \frac{T_f}{T_i} & = & - \ln \frac{V_f}{V_i} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \left( \frac{T_f}{T_i} \right)^{\frac{C_V}{k_B}} & = & \frac{V_i}{V_f} \end{eqnarray} $

So the final volume, after the adiabatic compression, is uniquely determined.

$ \begin{eqnarray} T^{(\frac{C_V}{k_B})} V & = & constant \end{eqnarray} $

We can determine the final pressure by using the ideal gas law on this result. We could also have found the pressure-temperature relationship more directly using $d(PV) = N K_B dT$. In either case we get the results

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{1}{P} T^{(\frac{C_P}{k_B})} & = & constant \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} P V^{\gamma} & = & constant, \gamma = \frac{C_P}{C_V} \end{eqnarray} $

The work done $W$ is the change in thermal energy. We can now complete the first row of our table.

Isothermal Expansion

The heat engine is now placed in contact with the hot resevoir. The piston is allowed to move (by slowly unloading the force on the piston). Since the change is isothermal, the governing equation is

$ \begin{eqnarray} P V & = & constant \end{eqnarray} $

In the isothermal expansion, the final pressure-volume combination is not uniquely determined because any pairing will do as long as the temperature is held constant. The final pressure, or volume, will depend upon how much we unload the piston. If we assume that the final pressure is $\alpha P_0$ then we are being completely general. In realistic engines, $\alpha$ may be close to unity.

Since the gas is expanding, it will do work on the pistion. However, to keep our signs straight we will compute the work done on the gas by the piston, which we expect to be negative!

$ \begin{eqnarray} dW & = & -P dV & = & - \left( \frac{N k_B T}{V} \right) dV \end{eqnarray} $

Because the temperature is constant, this can be easily integrated.

$ \begin{eqnarray} W & = & -P dV & = & - N k_B T \ln \left(\frac{V_f}{V_i} \right) \end{eqnarray} $

Isothermal Compression

We can see that we shall need to compress the gas to restore the original pressure and volume. Since $PV = constant$, we will be able to adjust the loading on the pistion to restore the original pressure and volume exactly.

Efficiency

The heat flowing from the hot resevoir to the engine is

$ \begin{eqnarray} Q_H & = & +N k_B T_H (\frac{C_P}{k_B} \ln \frac{T_H}{T_C} - \ln \alpha) \end{eqnarray} $

How much of this gets converted to work done by the gas? The work done by the gas is the negative of the work done on the gas over the entire cycle. The contributions from the adiabatic changes cancel exactly.

$ \begin{eqnarray} W & = & N k_B T_H (\ln \frac{1}{\alpha} + \frac{C_P}{k_B} \ln \frac{T_H}{T_C}) + N k_B T_C (\ln \alpha - \frac{C_P}{k_B} \ln \frac{T_H}{T_C}) \\ & = & N k_B (T_H - T_C) (\frac{C_P}{k_B} \ln \frac{T_H}{T_C} - \ln \alpha) \end{eqnarray} $

We see that the efficiency of the Carnot engine is

$ \begin{eqnarray} e_{C} & = & \frac{W}{Q_H} & = & \frac{T_{H} - T_{C}}{T_{H}} & = & 1 - \frac{T_{C}}{T_{H}} \end{eqnarray} $ where

  • $e_{C}$ is the thermal efficiency of the Carnot engine
  • $T_{H}$ is the temperature of the input gas
  • $T_{C}$ is the temperature of the exhaust gas

The efficiency does not even depend upon how much work we try to extract per cycle by varying $\alpha$. The efficiency only depends on the temperatures of the hot and cold resevoirs.

Statistical Mechanics

All microstates corresponding to a given macrostate are equally probable.

Distributing $q$ Quanta among $N$ Oscillators

This is equivalent to the problem of computing the number of color arrangements of objects drawn from a bag where the objects come in two colors.

Consider $q$ quanta and $N-1$ boundaries. Start by thinking of them as $q+N-1$ distinguishable objects and then correct for the separate indistinguishability of the quanta and boundaries.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Omega & = & \frac{(q + N - 1)!}{q!(N-1)!} \end{eqnarray} $

Thermal Equilibrium of Blocks in Contact

The equilibrium macrostate corresponds to the most probable microstate.

The distribution of the number of ways is very sharply peaked.

Definition of Entropy

I don't like this being introduced without motivation.

$ \begin{eqnarray} S & = & k_B \ln \Omega \end{eqnarray} $

The Boltzmann constant is $k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} J/K$.

Entropy has units joules per kelvin.

Condition for Thermal Equilibrium

In equilibrium, the most probable energy distribution is the one that maximizes the total entropy.

Second Law of Thermodynamics

In a closed system that is not in equilibrium, the most probable consequence is that the entropy of the system will increase.

I find this a bit difficult to accept without some statement about how accessible certain microstates are.

Reversible and Irreversible Processes

A reversible process is one in which the total entropy change is zero.

An irreversible process is one in which the total entropy increases.

There is nothing to stop the total entropy of a closed system from going down, it's just very unlikely. So an irreversible process may reverse in principle (it does not violate the conservation laws of physics), but is extremely unlikely to do so.

Temperature

At equilibrium, the total entropy $S = S_1 + S_2$ is maximized with respect to any heat energy transfer.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial S}{\partial q_1} & = & \frac{\partial S_1}{\partial q_1} + \frac{\partial S_2}{\partial q_1} = 0 \end{eqnarray} $

But since $dq_1 = -dq_2$, we have

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial S_1}{\partial q_1} & = & \frac{\partial S_2}{\partial q_2} \end{eqnarray} $

So temperature is some function of $\partial S / \partial Q$.

But heat flows from hot to cold and to the object whose entropy increases more, so

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{1}{T} & = & \frac{\partial S}{\partial Q} \end{eqnarray} $
