Rigid Body Motion

Fixed Point of Rotation

A fixed point of rotation means that the distance from the origin for every particle is constant in time.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d (\vec{x}_{i})^{2}}{dt} & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Then $\vec{x}_{i}$ must be perpendicular to the velocity so we can write

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d \vec{x}_{i}}{dt} & = & \vec{\omega}_{i} \times \vec{x}_{i} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

where the $\vec{\omega}_{i}$ is not unique (add multiple of the position vector).

Rigid Body Definition

A rigid body is one in which the distance between any pair of particles is constant in time.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d (\vec{x}_{i} - \vec{x}_{j})^{2}}{dt} & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Then

$ \begin{eqnarray} (\vec{x}_{i} - \vec{x}_{j}) \cdot (\frac{d \vec{x}_{i}}{dt} - \frac{d \vec{x}_{j}}{dt}) & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Rotation Kinematics

The angular velocity is defined as

$ \begin{eqnarray} \omega & = & \frac{d \theta}{d t} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

For a rigid body rotating around a fixed point (combining the previous two definitions) we can show that a rigid body rotating about a fixed origin has a vector property, $\vec{\omega}$, such that for any particle in the body

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d \vec{x}_{i}}{dt} & = & \vec{\omega} \times \vec{x}_{i} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

The angular acceleration is defined as

$ \begin{eqnarray} \alpha & = & \frac{d \omega}{d t} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

The magnitude of the linear velocity is then related to the angular velocity by

$ \begin{eqnarray} v & = & r \omega \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

By considering the motion of a particle in a circular orbit with uniform angular velocity, the acceleration is found to be directed towards the center. This centripetal acceleration is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{a}_{c} & = & - \omega^{2} \vec{r} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

The tangential acceleration is found to be

$ \begin{eqnarray} a_{t} & = & r \alpha \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

Uniformly accelerated rotational motion

Integrating the kinematic equations gives

$ \begin{eqnarray} \omega & = & \omega_{0} + \alpha t \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \theta - \theta_{0} & = & \omega_{0} t + \frac{1}{2} \alpha t^{2} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \omega^{2} & = & \omega_{0}^2 + 2 \alpha (\theta - \theta_{0}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

Rotational Motion Dynamics

A torque produces an angular acceleration.

The torque, $\vec{T}$, of a force, $\vec{F}$, applied a displacement $\vec{r}$ from the origin is defined as

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{T} & = & \vec{r} \times \vec{F} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

Rotational Inertia

What is the rotational analog of inertial mass? Using the fact that a rigid body rotating about a fixed origin has the unique vector property, $\vec{\omega}$, that connects the velocity and position of any particle, we can compute the angular momentum of the rigid body as follows

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{L} & = & \sum_{i} \vec{x}_{i} \times \vec{p}_{i} \\ & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} \vec{x}_{i} \times \vec{v}_{i} \\ & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} \vec{x}_{i} \times (\omega \times \vec{x}_{i}) \\ & = & \sum_{i} (m_{i} (\vec{x}_{i})^2 \vec{\omega} - m_{i} (\vec{\omega} \cdot \vec{x}_{i})\vec{x}_{i}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

In general, the angular momentum and the angular velocity are not parallel and we are forced to define an Inertia tensor. Alternatively, we can say that the angular momentum is computed from a linear transformation of the angular velocity. In the special cases where the mass of the rigid body lies in a plane or where there is symmetry above and below a plane, the second term is zero.

In such special cases,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{L} & = & I \vec{\omega} \\ \end{eqnarray} $
