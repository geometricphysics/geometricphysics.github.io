Rigid Body Motion Moment of Inertia

Introduction

From studying a general multiparticle system, we now ask what happens when the relative motions of the particles are constrained. One of the major concepts that emerges is a linear function of the angular velocity called the moment of inertia.

Fixed Point of Rotation

A fixed point of rotation means that the distance from the origin for every particle is constant in time.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d (\Vector{x}_{i})^{2}}{dt} & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Then $\Vector{x}_{i}$ must be perpendicular to the velocity so we can write

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d \Vector{x}_{i}}{dt} & = & \Vector{\omega}_{i} \times \Vector{x}_{i} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

where the $\Vector{\omega}_{i}$ is not unique (we can add a multiple of the position vector).

With this 'feeble' constraint, every particle is moving on its own sphere, not constrained to a plane. It's rather elegant though that forcing the velocity vector to be orthogonal to the position vector constrains each particle to move on the surface of a sphere.

Rigid Body Definition

A rigid body is one in which the distance between any pair of particles is constant in time.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d (\Vector{x}_{i} - \Vector{x}_{j})^{2}}{dt} & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Then

$ \begin{eqnarray} (\Vector{x}_{i} - \Vector{x}_{j}) \cdot (\frac{d \Vector{x}_{i}}{dt} - \frac{d \Vector{x}_{j}}{dt}) & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Combining Rigid Body Definition

If we combine the fixed rotation point constraint with the rigid body constraint then we might now imagine some solid body spinning around a fixed point.

$ \begin{eqnarray} (\Vector{x}_i - \Vector{x}_j) \cdot (\dot{\Vector{x}}_i - \dot{\Vector{x}}_j) & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

Substitute expression for fixed point of rotation:

$ \begin{eqnarray} (\Vector{x}_i - \Vector{x}_j) \cdot (\omega_i \times \Vector{x}_i - \omega_j \times \Vector{x}_j) & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

Expand and observe that some terms are zero:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{x}_i \cdot (\omega_j \times \Vector{x}_j) + \Vector{x}_j \cdot (\omega_i \times \Vector{x}_i) & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

Cyclically permute the triple scalar product:

$ \begin{eqnarray} (\omega_i - \omega_j) \cdot (\Vector{x}_i \times \Vector{x}_j) & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

Deduce that the form of the $\Vector{\omega}_i$ must be:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{\omega}_i & = & \Vector{\omega} + \lambda_i \Vector{x}_i \end{eqnarray} $

Here $\Vector{\omega}$ is independent of $i$ and is a property of the system.

The motion of every particle is now described by

$ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{\Vector{x}}_i & = & \Vector{\omega} \times \Vector{x}_i \end{eqnarray} $

Rotation Kinematics

We digress for a moment for a more elementary treatment in Cartesian coordinates.

Consider a particle rotating in a circle with

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{x} & = & R \cos \theta(t) \UnitVector{i} + R \sin \theta(t) \UnitVector{j} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The angular velocity vector is defined as

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{\omega}(t) & = & \frac{d \theta}{d t}(t) \UnitVector{k} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Since the particle moves in a circle, $R$ is constant in time. However, the angular velocity magnitude may depend on time.

Differentiating twice w.r.t time to obtain the acceleration

$ \begin{eqnarray} \ddot{\Vector{x}} & = & - \omega^{2} \Vector{x} + \frac{d \omega}{d t} \times \Vector{x} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

This allows us to identify both a centripetal acceleration as well as a tangential acceleration.

Using $\Vector{x} = R \UnitVector{x}$,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \ddot{\Vector{x}} & = & - \omega^{2} R \UnitVector{x} + \frac{d^2 \theta}{d t^2} R (\UnitVector{\omega} \times \UnitVector{x}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

For a rigid body rotating around a fixed point (combining the previous two definitions) we can show that a rigid body rotating about a fixed origin has a vector property, $\Vector{\omega}$, such that for any particle in the body

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d \Vector{x}_{i}}{dt} & = & \Vector{\omega} \times \Vector{x}_{i} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The angular acceleration is defined as

$ \begin{eqnarray} \alpha & = & \frac{d^2 \theta}{d t^2} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The magnitude of the linear velocity is then related to the angular velocity by

$ \begin{eqnarray} v & = & R \omega \\ \end{eqnarray} $

By considering the motion of a particle in a circular orbit with uniform angular velocity, the acceleration is found to be directed towards the center.

The centripetal acceleration is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{a}_{c} & = & - \omega^{2} \Vector{x} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The tangential acceleration is found to be

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{a}_{t} & = & R \alpha (\UnitVector{\omega} \times \UnitVector{x}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Uniformly accelerated rotational motion

Integrating the kinematic equations for a constant angular acceleration gives

$ \begin{eqnarray} \omega & = & \omega_{0} + \alpha t \\ \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \theta - \theta_{0} & = & \omega_{0} t + \frac{1}{2} \alpha t^{2} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \omega^{2} & = & \omega_{0}^2 + 2 \alpha (\theta - \theta_{0}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Rotational Motion Dynamics

A torque produces an angular acceleration.

The torque, $\Vector{T}$, of a force, $\Vector{F}$, applied a displacement $\Vector{r}$ from the origin is defined as

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{T} & = & \Vector{r} \times \Vector{F} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Rotational Inertia

What is the rotational analog of inertial mass? Using the fact that a rigid body rotating about a fixed origin has the unique vector property, $\Vector{\omega}$, that connects the velocity and position of any particle, we can compute the angular momentum of the rigid body as follows

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{L} & = & \sum_{i} \Vector{x}_{i} \times \Vector{p}_{i} \\ & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} \Vector{x}_{i} \times \Vector{v}_{i} \\ & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} \Vector{x}_{i} \times (\Vector{\omega} \times \Vector{x}_{i}) \\ & = & \sum_{i} (m_{i} (\Vector{x}_{i})^2 \Vector{\omega} - m_{i} (\Vector{\omega} \cdot \Vector{x}_{i})\Vector{x}_{i}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

In general, the angular momentum and the angular velocity are not parallel and we are forced to define an Inertia tensor. Alternatively, we can say that the angular momentum is computed from a linear transformation of the angular velocity

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{L} & = & \Vector{L}(\Vector{\omega}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

General Properties of $\Vector{L}(\Vector{\omega})$

The following properties of the angular momentum transformation follow from the the formula for $\vec{L}(\vec{\omega})$ and the conditions for a rigid body and a fixed rotation point.

Linear $ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{L}(\alpha^{i} \vec{\omega}_{i}) & = & \alpha^{i} \vec{L}(\vec{\omega}_{i}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $
Symmetric $ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{\omega_{1}} \cdot \vec{L}(\vec{\omega_{2}}) & = & \vec{L}(\vec{\omega}_{1}) \cdot \vec{\omega_{2}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $
Positive definite (when $\vec{\omega} \neq 0$) $ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{\omega} \cdot \vec{L}(\vec{\omega}) & > & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $
Kinematic $ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{\vec{L}}(\vec{b}) & = & \vec{\omega} \times \vec{L}(\vec{b}) + \vec{L}(\vec{b} \times \vec{\omega}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Symmetry

In the special cases where the mass of the rigid body lies in a plane or where there is symmetry above and below a plane or where there is axial symmetry, the second term has components only parallel to $\Vector{\omega}$. For example $\Vector{x}_1 = \Vector{x}$

In such special cases

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{L} & = & I_{symmetric} \, \Vector{\omega} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} I_{symmetric} & = & \sum_i m_i (\Vector{x}_i)^2 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Parallel-Axis Theorem

We now investigate the angular momentum when a different origin is used. Then by adding the conditions for a rigid body and rotation about the point we obtain a relation among the inertia tensors (angular momentum transformations).

Let $\Vector{X}_{CM}$ be the center of mass relative to some origin and let $A$ be the point with position vector $\Vector{a}$ relative to the same origin. We also define the displacement from $A$ to the center of mass

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{R} & = & \Vector{X}_{CM} - \Vector{a} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Let $\Vector{x}_{i}$ be the position vector from the origin to the $i$th particle with mass $m_{i}$. We define the displacement of each particle from the center of mass

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{r}_{i} & = & \Vector{x}_{i} - \Vector{X}_{CM} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Adding the previous two equations,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{r}_{i} + \Vector{R} & = & \Vector{x}_{i} - \Vector{a} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The angular momentum relative to the point $A$ of a system of particles is

Note how the momenta are calculated using a Galilean transformation of velocity relative to $A$. It's not that we are being non-relativistic. The point is that if $A$ is moving relative to the origin then the momenta of the particles relative to $A$ will change.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{L}_{A} & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} (\Vector{x}_{i} - \Vector{a}) \times (\dot{\Vector{x}}_{i} - \dot{\Vector{a}}) \\ & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} (\Vector{r}_{i} + \Vector{R}) \times (\dot{\Vector{r}}_{i} + \dot{\Vector{R}}) \\ & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} (\Vector{r}_{i} \times \dot{\Vector{r}}_{i}) + \Vector{R} \times \sum_i m_i \dot{\Vector{r}}_i - \dot{\Vector{R}} \times \sum_i m_i \Vector{r}_i + \Vector{R} \times \dot{\Vector{R}} \sum_i m_i \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The angular momentum relative to the center of mass of the same system of particles is

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{L}_{CM} & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} \Vector{r}_{i} \times \dot{\Vector{r}}_{i} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Using the fact that $\Vector{r}_i$ is a displacement from the center of mass we conclude that

$ \begin{eqnarray} \sum_{i} m_{i} \Vector{r}_{i} & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

And so

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{L}_{A} & = & \Vector{L}_{CM} + M \Vector{R} \times \dot{\Vector{R}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

This is a very general result because it allows the possibility that $A$ may be moving.

Now make $A$ the fixed rotation point and assume that the system of particles comprise a rigid body so that given $\Vector{s}_{i} = \Vector{x}_{i} - \Vector{a} = \Vector{r}_i + \Vector{R}$,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{\Vector{s}}_{i} & = & \Vector{\omega} \times \Vector{s}_{i} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

From this equation we can show (substitute $\Vector{s}_i = \Vector{r}_i + \Vector{R}$, multiply by $m_i$ and sum over $i$)

$ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{\Vector{R}} & = & \Vector{\omega} \times \Vector{R} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Finally, we obtain

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{L}_{A} & = & \Vector{L}_{CM} + M [ R^{2} \Vector{\omega} - (\Vector{R} \cdot \Vector{\omega}) \Vector{R} ] \\ \end{eqnarray} $

This is the parallel-axis theorem in its more general form. It is normally derived under the simplification that the angular momentum is parallel to the angular velocity, in which case the inertia tensor becomes a scalar multiplier - the moment of inertia, and the theorem is presented as a relation among scalar values:

$ \begin{eqnarray} I_{A} & = & I_{CM} + M R^{2} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Kinetic Energy of Rotation

An easy route to the kinetic energy of a rotating body is by applying a few vector identities.

$ \begin{eqnarray} K & = & \sum_i \frac{1}{2} m_i \Vector{v_i}^2 \end{eqnarray} $

The rigid body condition amounts to all particles sharing the same angular velocity $\Vector{\omega}$, with $\Vector{v_i} = \Vector{\omega} \times \Vector{r_i}$.

Substitute this into our expression for the kinetic energy and pull the factor of $1/2$ out front.

$ \begin{eqnarray} K & = & \frac{1}{2} \sum_i m_i \Vector{v_i} \cdot (\Vector{\omega} \times \Vector{r_i}) \end{eqnarray} $

Cyclically permute the triple scalar product to get the angular velocity out in front.

$ \begin{eqnarray} K & = & \frac{1}{2} \Vector{\omega} \cdot \sum_i (\Vector{r_i} \times m_i \Vector{v_i}) \end{eqnarray} $

Recognize that the sum is the angular momentum.

$ \begin{eqnarray} K & = & \frac{1}{2} \Vector{\omega} \cdot \Vector{L}(\Vector{\omega}) \end{eqnarray} $

Under the usual symmetry conditions for the moment of inertia tensor, this simplifies to

$ \begin{eqnarray} K & = & \frac{1}{2} I \Vector{\omega}^2 \end{eqnarray} $

It's easy to differentiate this scalar equation to show $\dot{K} = \omega \Gamma$. With a bit mork work we can find the more general expression when the moment of inertia is a tensor.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d}{d t}(\frac{1}{2} \Vector{\omega} \cdot \Vector{L}(\Vector{\omega})) & = & \frac{1}{2} \dot{\Vector{\omega}} \cdot \Vector{L}(\Vector{\omega}) + \frac{1}{2} \Vector{\omega} \cdot \dot{\Vector{L}}(\Vector{\omega}) \\ & = & \frac{1}{2} \Vector{\omega} \cdot \Vector{L}(\Vector{\dot{\omega}}) + \frac{1}{2} \Vector{\omega} \cdot \dot{\Vector{L}}(\Vector{\omega}) \end{eqnarray} $

The last step follows from the Symmetric property of the angular momentum. By differentiating $\Vector{L}$ it is possible to identify $\Vector{L}(\dot{\omega})$ as one of the resulting terms. Other terms are zero because $\dot{\Vector{x_i}} = \Vector{\omega} \times \Vector{x_i}$. We'll do this step explicitely because there are some surprising cancellations:

$ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{\Vector{L}}(\vectorOmega) & = & \sum_i m_i \left[2 (\vectorX_i \cdot \dot{\vectorX_i}) \vectorOmega + (\vectorX_i)^2 \dot{\vectorOmega} - (\dot{\vectorOmega} \cdot \vectorX_i) \vectorX_i - (\vectorOmega \cdot \dot{\vectorX_i}) \vectorX_i - (\vectorOmega \cdot \vectorX_i) \dot{\vectorX_i} \right] \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The first and fourth term vanish because $\dot{\vectorX_i} = \vectorOmega \cross \vectorX_i$. The last term will vanish upon taking the scalar product with $\vectorOmega$. What remains is $\vectorBigL(\dot{\vectorOmega})$. So we have the identity $\vectorOmega \cdot \dot{\vectorBigL}(\vectorOmega) = \vectorOmega \cdot \vectorBigL(\dot{\vectorOmega})$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d}{d t}(\frac{1}{2} \Vector{\omega} \cdot \Vector{L}(\Vector{\omega})) & = & \frac{1}{2} \Vector{\omega} \cdot \Vector{L}(\Vector{\dot{\omega}}) + \frac{1}{2} \Vector{\omega} \cdot \dot{\Vector{L}}(\Vector{\omega}) \\ & = & \vectorOmega \cdot \dot{\vectorBigL}(\vectorOmega) \\ & = & \vectorOmega \cdot \vectorBigN \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The quantity

$ \begin{eqnarray} K_{rot} & = & \frac{1}{2} \vec{\omega} \cdot \vec{L}(\vec{\omega}) \end{eqnarray} $

is the rotational kinetic energy.

Of course, we should show that the rotational kinetic energy about the center of mass splits of from the translational kinetic energy of the center of mass.
