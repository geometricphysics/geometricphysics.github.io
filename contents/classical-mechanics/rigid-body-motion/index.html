Rigid Body Motion

Fixed Point of Rotation

A fixed point of rotation means that the distance from the origin for every particle is constant in time.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d (\vec{x}_{i})^{2}}{dt} & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Then $\vec{x}_{i}$ must be perpendicular to the velocity so we can write

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d \vec{x}_{i}}{dt} & = & \vec{\omega}_{i} \times \vec{x}_{i} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

where the $\vec{\omega}_{i}$ is not unique (add multiple of the position vector).

Rigid Body Definition

A rigid body is one in which the distance between any pair of particles is constant in time.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d (\vec{x}_{i} - \vec{x}_{j})^{2}}{dt} & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Then

$ \begin{eqnarray} (\vec{x}_{i} - \vec{x}_{j}) \cdot (\frac{d \vec{x}_{i}}{dt} - \frac{d \vec{x}_{j}}{dt}) & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Rotation Kinematics

Consider a particle rotating in a circle with

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{x} & = & R cos \theta \hat{i} + R sin \theta \hat{j} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

The angular velocity is defined as

$ \begin{eqnarray} \omega & = & \frac{d \theta}{d t} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

Since the particle moves in a circle, $R$ is constant in time. However, the angular velocity may depend on time.

Differentiating twice w.r.t time to obtain the acceleration

$ \begin{eqnarray} \ddot{\vec{x}} & = & - \omega^{2} R \hat{x} + \frac{d \omega}{d t} R (\hat{\omega} \times \hat{x}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

This allows us to identify both a centripetal acceleration as well as a tangential acceleration.

For a rigid body rotating around a fixed point (combining the previous two definitions) we can show that a rigid body rotating about a fixed origin has a vector property, $\vec{\omega}$, such that for any particle in the body

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d \vec{x}_{i}}{dt} & = & \vec{\omega} \times \vec{x}_{i} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

The angular acceleration is defined as

$ \begin{eqnarray} \alpha & = & \frac{d \omega}{d t} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

The magnitude of the linear velocity is then related to the angular velocity by

$ \begin{eqnarray} v & = & r \omega \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

By considering the motion of a particle in a circular orbit with uniform angular velocity, the acceleration is found to be directed towards the center. This centripetal acceleration is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{a}_{c} & = & - \omega^{2} \vec{r} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

The tangential acceleration is found to be

$ \begin{eqnarray} a_{t} & = & r \alpha \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

Uniformly accelerated rotational motion

Integrating the kinematic equations gives

$ \begin{eqnarray} \omega & = & \omega_{0} + \alpha t \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \theta - \theta_{0} & = & \omega_{0} t + \frac{1}{2} \alpha t^{2} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \omega^{2} & = & \omega_{0}^2 + 2 \alpha (\theta - \theta_{0}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

Rotational Motion Dynamics

A torque produces an angular acceleration.

The torque, $\vec{T}$, of a force, $\vec{F}$, applied a displacement $\vec{r}$ from the origin is defined as

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{T} & = & \vec{r} \times \vec{F} \\ \end{eqnarray} $.

Rotational Inertia

What is the rotational analog of inertial mass? Using the fact that a rigid body rotating about a fixed origin has the unique vector property, $\vec{\omega}$, that connects the velocity and position of any particle, we can compute the angular momentum of the rigid body as follows

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{L} & = & \sum_{i} \vec{x}_{i} \times \vec{p}_{i} \\ & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} \vec{x}_{i} \times \vec{v}_{i} \\ & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} \vec{x}_{i} \times (\omega \times \vec{x}_{i}) \\ & = & \sum_{i} (m_{i} (\vec{x}_{i})^2 \vec{\omega} - m_{i} (\vec{\omega} \cdot \vec{x}_{i})\vec{x}_{i}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

In general, the angular momentum and the angular velocity are not parallel and we are forced to define an Inertia tensor. Alternatively, we can say that the angular momentum is computed from a linear transformation of the angular velocity

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{L} & = & \vec{L}(\vec{\omega}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

General Properties of $\vec{L}(\vec{\omega})$

The following properties of the angular momentum transformation follow from the the formula for $\vec{L}(\vec{\omega})$ and the conditions for a rigid body and a fixed rotation point.

Linear $ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{L}(\alpha^{i} \vec{\omega}_{i}) & = & \alpha^{i} \vec{L}(\vec{\omega}_{i}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $
Symmetric $ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{\omega_{1}} \cdot \vec{L}(\vec{\omega_{2}}) & = & \vec{L}(\vec{\omega}_{1}) \cdot \vec{\omega_{2}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $
Positive definite (when $\vec{\omega} \neq 0$) $ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{\omega} \cdot \vec{L}(\vec{\omega}) & > & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $
Kinematic $ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{\vec{L}}(\vec{b}) & = & \vec{\omega} \times \vec{L}(\vec{b}) + \vec{L}(\vec{b} \times \vec{\omega}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Symmetry

In the special cases where the mass of the rigid body lies in a plane or where there is symmetry above and below a plane or where there is axial symmetry, the second term is zero.

In such special cases,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{L} & = & I \, \vec{\omega} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Parallel-Axis Theorem

We now investigate the angular momentum when a different origin is used. Then by adding the conditions for a rigid body and rotation about the point we obtain a relation among the inertia tensors (angular momentum.transformations).

Let $\vec{X}_{CM}$ be the center of mass relative to some origin and let $Y$ be the point with position vector $\vec{Y}$ relative to the same origin. We also define the displacement from $Y$ to the center of mass

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{R} & = & \vec{X}_{CM} - \vec{Y} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Let $\vec{x}_{i}$ be the position vector from the origin to the $i$th particle with mass $m_{i}$. We define the displacement of each particle from the center of mass

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{r}_{i} & = & \vec{x}_{i} - \vec{X}_{CM} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The angular momentum relative to the point $Y$ of a system of particles is

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{L}_{Y} & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} (\vec{x}_{i} - \vec{Y}) \times (\dot{\vec{x}}_{i} - \dot{\vec{Y}}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The angular momentum relative to the center of mass of the same system of particles is

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{L}_{CM} & = & \sum_{i} m_{i} \vec{r}_{i} \times \dot{\vec{x}}_{i} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Using the fact that $\vec{r}_i$ is a displacement from the center of mass we conclude that

$ \begin{eqnarray} \sum_{i} m_{i} \vec{r}_{i} & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Expanding the expression for $\vec{L}_{Y}$ with $\vec{x}_{i} - \vec{Y} = \vec{r}_{i} + \vec{R}$ and using the previous observation we obtain

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{L}_{Y} & = & \vec{L}_{CM} + M \vec{R} \times \dot{\vec{R}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Now make $Y$ the fixed rotation point and assume that the system of particles comprise a rigid body so that given $\vec{s}_{i} = \vec{x}_{i} - \vec{Y}$,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{\vec{s}}_{i} & = & \omega \times \vec{s}_{i} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

From this equation we can show

$ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{\vec{R}} & = & \omega \times \vec{R} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Finally, we obtain

$ \begin{eqnarray} \vec{L}_{Y} & = & \vec{L}_{CM} + M [ R^{2} \vec{\omega} - (\vec{R} \cdot \vec{\omega}) \vec{R} ] \\ \end{eqnarray} $

This is the parallel-axis theorem in its more general form. It is normally derived under the simplification that the angular momentum is parallel to the angular velocity, in which case the inertia tensor becomes a scalar multiplier - the moment of inertia, and the theorem is presented as a relation among scalar values:

$ \begin{eqnarray} I_{Y} & = & I_{CM} + M R^{2} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Kinetic Energy of Rotation

Applying the general properties of the angular momentum linear transformation we find

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d}{d t}(\frac{1}{2} \vec{\omega} \cdot \vec{L}(\vec{\omega})) & = & \vec{\omega} \cdot \frac{d}{d t} \vec{L}(\vec{\omega}) \\ & = & \vec{\omega} \cdot \vec{N} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The quantity

$ \begin{eqnarray} K_{rot} & = & \frac{1}{2} \vec{\omega} \cdot \vec{L}(\vec{\omega}) \end{eqnarray} $

is the rotational kinetic energy.
