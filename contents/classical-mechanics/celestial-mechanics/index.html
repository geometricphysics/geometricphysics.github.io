Celestial Mechanics

Earth's motion around the Sun, not as simple as I thought

Kepler Motion and Conic Orbits using Newton's Equations

It is common to treat Kepler Motion using Lagrange's Equations. Here we use only basic trigonometry and single variable calculus to obtain the same results.

We'll introduce the coordinate transformation between Cartesian coordinates and Polar coordinates. We'll perform differentiation in Cartesian coordinates because the unit vectors are constant and then transform the acceleration to Polar coordinates. We will then consider a central force.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{r} & = & x \UnitVector{i} + y \UnitVector{j} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} x & = & r \cos \theta \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} y & = & r \sin \theta \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{r} & = & r ( \cos \theta \UnitVector{i} + \sin \theta \UnitVector{j} ) \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \UnitVector{r} & = & ( \cos \theta \UnitVector{i} + \sin \theta \UnitVector{j} ) \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \UnitVector{\theta} & = & ( \cos \theta \UnitVector{j} - \sin \theta \UnitVector{i} ) \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{\UnitVector{r}} & = & \dot{\theta} \UnitVector{\theta} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{\UnitVector{\theta}} & = & - \dot{\theta} \UnitVector{r} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{\Vector{r}} & = & \dot{r} \UnitVector{r} + r \dot{\theta} \UnitVector{\theta} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \ddot{\Vector{r}} & = & [\ddot{r}- r\dot{\theta}^2]\UnitVector{r}+[2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}+r\ddot{\theta}]\UnitVector{\theta} \end{eqnarray} $

The radial force we shall denote as $F_r$, so with $\mu$ the reduced mass,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \mu ( \ddot{r}- r\dot{\theta}^2) & = & F_r \end{eqnarray} $

The tangential force is zero

$ \begin{eqnarray} 2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}+r\ddot{\theta} & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

This equation expresses the conservation of angular momentum. The magnitude of the angular momentum $l$ is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} l & = & \mu r^2 \dot{\theta} \end{eqnarray} $

Now consider the reciprocal of the radial distance $r$

$ \begin{eqnarray} u & = & \frac{1}{r} \end{eqnarray} $

Differentiating with respect to $\theta$ gives

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{du}{d \theta} & = & - \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{dr}{d \theta} & = & - \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{dr}{dt} \frac{dt}{d \theta} & = & - \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{dr}{dt} \frac{dt}{d \theta} & = & - \frac{\dot{r}}{r^2 \dot{\theta}} & = & - \frac{\mu \dot{r}}{l} \end{eqnarray} $

Differentiating a second time with respect to $\theta$ gives

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d^2u}{d \theta^2} & = & - \frac{\mu \ddot{r}}{\dot{\theta}l} & = & - \frac{F_r + \mu r \dot{\theta}^2}{\dot{\theta}l} & = & - \frac{\mu r^2 F_r}{l^2} - \frac{1}{r} \end{eqnarray} $

Finally, expressing this result in terms of $u$

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d^2u}{d \theta^2} + u & = & - \frac{\mu F_r}{l^2 u^2} \end{eqnarray} $

For an inverse square law

$ \begin{eqnarray} F_r & = & \frac{\kappa}{r^2} \end{eqnarray} $

Hence

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d^2u}{d \theta^2} + u & = & - \frac{\mu \kappa}{l^2} \end{eqnarray} $

This equation can be integrated to give

$ \begin{eqnarray} u & = & \frac{1}{r} = - \frac{\mu \kappa}{l^2} [1 - e \cos(\theta -\theta_0)] \end{eqnarray} $

Thus, the equation of a body under a central force is a conic section. This is the generalized form of Kepler's First Law. For a planet, $\kappa \lt 0$, and $e \lt 1$. The orbit of a planet is an ellipse with the sun at the focus.

The angular momentum is related to the rate at which the radius vector sweeps out area

$ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{A} & = & \frac{1}{2} \Norm{\Vector{r} \times \Vector{v}} & = & \frac{1}{2} \Norm{r\UnitVector{r} \times (\dot{r} \UnitVector{r} + r \dot{\theta} \UnitVector{\theta})} & = & \frac{r^2 \dot{\theta}}{2} & = & \frac{l}{2 \mu} \end{eqnarray} $

This is Kepler's Second Law. The radius vector sweeps out equal areas in equal times.

We now consider the period of the orbit. We now require some results for ellipses and orbits. The area of the ellipse, $A$ is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} A & = & \pi a b & = & \pi a a^{\frac{1}{2}} l^{\frac{1}{2}} & = & \pi a^{\frac{3}{2}} l (\frac{-1}{\mu \kappa})^{\frac{1}{2}} \end{eqnarray} $

The period $T$ is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} T & = & \frac{A}{\dot{A}} & = & 2 \pi (\frac{\mu}{-\kappa})^{\frac{1}{2}} a^{\frac{3}{2}} \end{eqnarray} $

Inserting

$ \begin{eqnarray} \kappa & = & - G M m \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \mu & = & \frac{M m}{M + m} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} T & = & 2 \pi (\frac{1}{G(M+m)})^{\frac{1}{2}} a^{\frac{3}{2}} \end{eqnarray} $

As long as $M \gg m$, we have Kepler's Third Law. The sqaure of the periods of the planets are proportional to the cubes of the major semi-axis.

Energy, Angular Momentum, and Eccentricity

The total energy (excluding the energy of the center of mass) is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} E & = & T + V = \frac{1}{2} \mu (\dot{r}^2 + r^2 \dot{\theta}^2) + \frac{\kappa}{r} \end{eqnarray} $

Substituting in the expression for the angular momentum we obtain

$ \begin{eqnarray} E & = & \frac{1}{2} \mu \dot{r}^2 + \frac{1}{2} \frac{l^2}{\mu r^2} + \frac{\kappa}{r} \end{eqnarray} $

It is easy to verify by differentiating that E is a constant of the motion, $\dot{E} = 0$.

We can relate the eccentricity $e$ to the energy $E$ and angular momentum $l$ by consirdering the apsidal distances when $\dot{r} = 0$. For the orbit equation we conclude (after differentiating wrt $t$ and setting $\dot{r} = 0$) that

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{1}{r} & = & \frac{\mu \kappa}{l^2} (e - 1) \end{eqnarray} $

Remark: In the case $e \lt 1$ we actually get two solutions. Here, I am considering the hyperbolic case.

Setting $\dot{r} = 0$ in the equation for $E$ and substituting the expression for $r$ we get

$ \begin{eqnarray} E & = & \frac{1}{2} \frac{\mu \kappa^2}{l^2} (e^2-1) \end{eqnarray} $

Solving for $e$ we obtain

$ \begin{eqnarray} e & = & \left(1 + \frac{2 E l^2}{\mu \kappa^2}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} \end{eqnarray} $
