Poisson Brackets

Definition of the Poisson Bracket

$ \begin{eqnarray} \{ A, B \} & = & \sum_{i} \left( \frac{\partial A}{\partial q_{i}} \frac{\partial B}{\partial p_{i}} - \frac{\partial A}{\partial p_{i}} \frac{\partial B}{\partial q_{i}} \right) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Fundamental Properties of Poisson Brackets

  1. Antisymmetry

    $ \begin{eqnarray} \{ A, C \} & = & - \{ C, A \} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

  2. Linearity

    $ \begin{eqnarray} \{ k A, B \} & = & k \{ A, B \} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

    $ \begin{eqnarray} \{ (A+B), C \} & = & \{ A, C \} + \{ B, C \} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

  3. Triple Product

    $ \begin{eqnarray} \{ (AB), C \} & = & B \{ A, C \} + A \{ B, C \} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

  4. Special PB's

    $ \begin{eqnarray} \{ q_{i}, q_{j} \} & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

    $ \begin{eqnarray} \{ p_{i}, p_{j} \} & = & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} $

    $ \begin{eqnarray} \{ q_{i}, p_{j} \} & = & \delta_{ij} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Derived properties of Poisson Brackets

We can now use the fundamental properties to derive further properties.

The following formula can be proved by induction.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \{ q^{n}, p \} & = & n q^{n - 1} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Using the fact that $n q^{n - 1}$ is simply a derivative,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \{ q^{n}, p \} & = & \frac{d (q^{n})}{d q} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Now take any polynomial of $q$, Using linearity we can show

$ \begin{eqnarray} \{ F(q), p \} & = & \frac{d F(q)}{d q} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Further,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \{ F(q, p), p_{i} \} & = & \frac{\partial F(q)}{\partial q_{i}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

And

$ \begin{eqnarray} \{ F(q, p), q_{i} \} & = & - \frac{\partial F(q)}{\partial p_{i}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $
