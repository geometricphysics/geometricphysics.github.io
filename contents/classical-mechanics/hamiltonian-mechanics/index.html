Hamiltonian Mechanics

Energy Conservation

Suppose the Lagrangian contains an explicit time dependence

$ \begin{eqnarray} L & = & L(q_{i}, \dot{q_{i}}, t) \end{eqnarray} $

Consider how $L$ changes as the system evolves

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d L}{d t} & = & \sum_{i} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_{i}} \dot{q_{i}} + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_{i}} \ddot{q_{i}} \right) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial t} \end{eqnarray} $

Using the usual substitutions for the generalized momentum and the Euler-Lagrange equations

$ \begin{eqnarray} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial t} & = & \frac{d}{d t} \left[ \sum_{i} p_{i} \dot{q_{i}} - L \right] \end{eqnarray} $

The quantity $H$ is the Hamiltonian of the system

$ \begin{eqnarray} H & = & \sum_{i} p_{i} \dot{q_{i}} - L \end{eqnarray} $

$H$ is the energy of the system.

If a system is time-translation invariant, then the quantity $H$ is conserved.

Phase Space and Hamilton's Equations

In the Lagrangian/Action formulation we focus on configuration space and the equations are second-order differential (Euler-Lagrange) equations.

In the Hamiltonian formulation we focus on phase space; the corrdinates $q_{i}$ and conjugate momenta $p_{i}$. While the number of dimensions is doubled, the equations of motion become first-order

$ \begin{eqnarray} H & = & H(q_{i}, p_{i}) \\ \dot{p_{i}} & = & - \frac{\partial H}{\partial q_{i}} \\ \dot{q_{i}} & = & \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_{i}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Harmonic Oscillator Hamiltonian

$ \begin{eqnarray} L & = & \frac{1}{2} m v^{2} - \frac{1}{2} k x^{2} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} q & = & (k m)^{\frac{1}{4}} x \\ \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} H & = & \frac{\omega}{2} \left( p^{2} + q^{2} \right) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \omega & = & (\frac{k}{m})^{\frac{1}{2}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The motion of the system in phase space is a circle.
