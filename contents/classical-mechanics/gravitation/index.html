Gravitation

Introduction

Isaac Newton was prompted to produce a mathematical description for planetary motion by Robert Hooke. Hooke suggested that planets may effectively perform elliptic orbits if their motion was a combination of straight tangential motion together with a falling towards the sun. Hooke was unable to complete the mathematical steps required. Newton accounted for Kepler's Laws with his Universal Law of Gravitation.

Orbits and Kepler's Laws

Kepler's First Law

Each planet's orbit about the Sun is an ellipse.

The Sun's center is always located at one focus of the orbital ellipse.

Kepler's Second Law

The imaginary line joining a planet and the Sun sweeps equal areas of space during equal time intervals as the planet orbits.

Kepler's Third Law

The squares of the orbital periods are directly proportional to the cubes of the semi-major axes of their orbits.

Deriving the Universal Law of Gravitation from Kepler's observations

Direction of the Gravitational Force

We show that Kepler's Second Law is obeyed if the force on the planet is directed towards the sun.

Let $\vec{r}$ be the displacement of the planet from the sun.

Suppose that the planet moves a small amount $\Delta \vec{r}$ in a time interval $\Delta t$.

Then the planet approximately sweeps out an area $\Delta A = \frac{1}{2} \vec{r} \wedge \Delta \vec{r}$ in time $\Delta t.$

Hence, $\Delta A / \Delta t = \frac{1}{2} \vec{r} \wedge \bar{v}$, where $\bar{v} = \Delta \vec{r} / \Delta t$ is the average velocity in the time interval $\Delta t$.

If we shrink the time interval $\Delta t$ we get $d A / d t = \frac{1}{2} \vec{r} \wedge \vec{v}$, for the rate of change of the area.

If the planet has mass $m$ and linear momentum $\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$, then the rate of change of area is

$dA / dt = \frac{1}{2m} L$, where $L = \vec{r} \wedge \vec{p}$ is the angular momentum of the planet about the sun.

So if we assume that the mass of the planet is constant, Kepler's Second Law is equivalent to the statement $dL / dt = 0$; the rate of change of angular momentum is zero, or the angular momentum is conserved in time.

But

$ \begin{eqnarray} 0 & = & dL / dt \\ & = & d (\vec{r} \wedge \vec{p}) / dt \\ & = & \vec{v} \wedge \vec{p} + \vec{r} \wedge \vec{F} \\ & = & \vec{r} \wedge \vec{F} \end{eqnarray} $

, where $\vec{F}$ is the force acting on the planet.

It follows from the properties of the wedge product that $\vec{F}$ must be a scalar multiple of $\vec{r}$.

The acceleration of the planet is in the direction of the force and is towards the sun. $\blacksquare$

The inverse-square dependence on separation

Kepler's Third Law is obeyed if the strength of the force is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between the particles.

The dependence on mass

The Law of Gravitation is said to be "Universal" because every massive particle is affected by the force and every massive particle creates a force around it.

The constant of proportionality: $G$

An experimental verification of the Gravitation law was first performed by Henry Cavendish. In addition, by controlling the masses and separation, the fundamental constant $G$ can be determined.

$G = 6.670 * 10^{11} newton • m^{2}/kg^{2}$
