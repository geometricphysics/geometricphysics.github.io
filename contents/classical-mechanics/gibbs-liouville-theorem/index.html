Gibbs-Liouville Theorem

Divergence in Phase Space

$ \begin{eqnarray} \nabla \cdot \vec{v} & = & \sum_{i} \left( \frac{\partial v_{q_{i}}}{\partial q_{i}} + \frac{\partial v_{p_{i}}}{\partial p_{i}} \right) \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} v_{q_{i}} & = & \dot{q_{i}} & = & \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_{i}} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} v_{p_{i}} & = & \dot{p_{i}} & = & \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_{i}} & = & - \frac{\partial H}{\partial q_{i}} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \nabla \cdot \vec{v} & = & \sum_{i} \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial q_{i}} \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_{i}} - \frac{\partial}{\partial p_{i}} \frac{\partial H}{\partial q_{i}} \right) \end{eqnarray} $

Becuase the order of partial differentiation does not matter,

$ \begin{eqnarray} \nabla \cdot \vec{v} & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

The volume occupied by a blob of phase space fluid is conserved with time.

Liouville's theorem is a close analogy to reversibility; the flux of $\vec{v}$ over any closed surface is zero. In quantum mechanics, Liouville's theorem is replaced by unitarity.

Poisson Brackets

Consider a field in phase space, $F(p, q)$. Along a trajectory, $F$ turns into a function of time.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \dot{F} & = & \sum_{i} \left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial p_{i}} \dot{p_{i}} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial q_{i}} \dot{q_{i}} \right) \\ & = & \sum_{i} \left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial p_{i}} \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_{i}} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial q_{i}} \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_{i}} \right) \\ & = & \sum_{i} \left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial q_{i}} \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_{i}} - \frac{\partial F}{\partial p_{i}} \frac{\partial H}{\partial q_{i}} \right) \\ & = & \{ F, H \} \end{eqnarray} $

The time derivative of any field along a trajectory is given by the Poisson bracket of that thing with the Hamiltonian.

The Poisson bracket even captures Hamilton's equations (substitute $p_{k}$ or $q_{k}$ for $F$).
