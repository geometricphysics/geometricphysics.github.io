Magnetostatics

Historical Development

Force per unit length between two parallel conducting wires is determined empirically to depend upon the currens in the wires and their separation.

$ \begin{eqnarray} F / length & = & \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2 \pi r} \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \mu_0 & = & 4 \pi \times 10^{-7} N / A^2 \end{eqnarray} $

Experiments involving a compass and a wire suggest a picture of circles of magnetic field surrounding a current-carrying wire and yet the magnetic force is perpendicular to the lines of $\Vector{B}$. Of course, a vectoral field is not the only possibility (bivector is more appropriate).

Considering an infinitesimally small length of wire $d\Vector{x}$, and accounting for the directions of the wire and magnetic field, the force is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} d \Vector{F}_{magnetic} & = & I \, d \Vector{x} \times \Vector{B} \end{eqnarray} $

A microscopic analysis of a current as a stream of charged particles suggests a magnetic force law for a moving charge and the introduction of a magnetic field $\Vector{B}$.

Consider a wire of cross-sectional area $A$ containing a current with drift velocity $\Vector{v}$ of charge carriers of density $n$ per unit volume and individual charge $q$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} I & = & n A v q \end{eqnarray} $

We deduce the magnetic force per charge to be

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{F}_{magnetic} & = & q (\Vector{v} \times \Vector{B}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Studies of various geometries of currents leads to a guess (Biot-Savart Law) for the connection between the magnetic field $\Vector{B}$ and the source current densities $\Vector{j}(\Vector{r})$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{B}(\Vector{r}) & = & \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} \int_{V} d^3\Primed{x} \frac{\Vector{j}(\Primed{\Vector{r}}) \times (\Vector{r} - \Primed{\Vector{r}})}{\Norm{\Vector{r} - \Primed{\Vector{r}}}^3} \end{eqnarray} $

The Biot Savart guess reproduces the observed static magnetic fields.

A consequence is that the magnetic field is divergence-free $\nabla \cdot \Vector{B} = 0$. In other words, there are no magnetic charges.

We also get Ampères Law

$ \begin{eqnarray} \oint_{C} d \Vector{x} \cdot \Vector{B} = \mu_0 I_{enclosed} \end{eqnarray} $

We can combine this with Stokes's theorem to obtain the differential form of Ampères Law (incomplete, as Maxwell observed, because it does not respect charge conservation).

$ \begin{eqnarray} \nabla \times \Vector{B} & = & \mu_0 \Vector{j} \end{eqnarray} $

Lorentz Force Law

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{F} & = & q (\Vector{E} + \Vector{v} \times \Vector{B}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $ where

  • $q$ is the charge of the particle
  • $\Vector{v}$ is the velocity of the particle
  • $\Vector{E}$ is the electric field
  • $\Vector{B}$ is the magnetic field

Magnetic forces alone cannot change the speed of a charged particle, they can only change the direction of motion.

Magnetic Field Units

The SI unit for the magnetic field is the tesla (abbreviated $T$) which is one newton per ampere-meter.

Another common unit is the gauss (abbreviated $G$) ($1 G = 10^{-4} T$).

Magnetic Force on a Current-Carrying Wire

As a consequence of the Lorentz Force Law and since $I = A v \rho = \lambda v$,

$ \begin{eqnarray} F & = & \lambda l v B sin \theta \\ F & = & B \, I \, l \, sin \theta \\ \end{eqnarray} $ where

  • $l$ is the length of the wire
  • $I$ is the current in the wire
  • $B$ is the magnetic field
  • $\theta$ is angle between $\Vector{B}$ and the wire

Magnetic Field due to a Current-Carrying Wire

Moving charges are the sources of magnetic fields.

$ \begin{eqnarray} B & = & \frac{\mu_{0} I}{2 \pi r} \\ \end{eqnarray} $ where

  • $\mu_{0}$ is permeability of free space ($4 \pi \times 10^{-7} N \cdot A^{2}$ or $4 \pi \times 10^{-7} T \cdot m / A$)

Grab the wire with the right hand and with the thumb in the direction of the conventional current. Fingers give the direction of the magnetic field.

Mass Spectrometer

A source of ions are accelerated from rest by a voltage $V$. The ions enter a region of uniform magnetic field perpendicular to their velocity. The ions execute a semi-circular path and their position of impact is recorded on photographic plates. Positively charged ions are called cations. Negatively charged ions are called anions.
