Magnetostatics

Lorentz Force Law

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{F} & = & q (\Vector{E} + \Vector{v} \times \Vector{B}) \\ \end{eqnarray} $ where

  • $q$ is the charge of the particle
  • $\Vector{v}$ is the velocity of the particle
  • $\Vector{E}$ is the electric field
  • $\Vector{B}$ is the magnetic field

Magnetic forces alone cannot change the speed of a charged particle, they can only change the direction of motion.

Magnetic Field Units

The SI unit for the magnetic field is the tesla (abbreviated $T$) which is one newton per ampere-meter.

Another common unit is the gauss (abbreviated $G$) ($1 G = 10^{-4} T$).

Magnetic Force on a Current-Carrying Wire

As a consequence of the Lorentz Force Law and since $I = A v \rho = \lambda v$,

$ \begin{eqnarray} F & = & \lambda l v B sin \theta \\ F & = & B \, I \, l \, sin \theta \\ \end{eqnarray} $ where

  • $l$ is the length of the wire
  • $I$ is the current in the wire
  • $B$ is the magnetic field
  • $\theta$ is angle between $\Vector{B}$ and the wire

Magnetic Field due to a Current-Carrying Wire

Moving charges are the sources of magnetic fields.

$ \begin{eqnarray} B & = & \frac{\mu_{0} I}{2 \pi r} \\ \end{eqnarray} $ where

  • $\mu_{0}$ is permeability of free space ($4 \pi \times 10^{-7} N \cdot A^{2}$ or $4 \pi \times 10^{-7} T \cdot m / A$)

Grab the wire with the right hand and with the thumb in the direction of the conventional current. Fingers give the direction of the magnetic field.

Mass Spectrometer

A source of ions are accelerated from rest by a voltage $V$. The ions enter a region of uniform magnetic field perpendicular to their velocity. The ions execute a semi-circular path and their position of impact is recorded on photographic plates. Positively charged ions are called cations. Negatively charged ions are called anions.
