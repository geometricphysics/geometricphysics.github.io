Electrostatics

Electric Charge

Studying electrodynamics begins with a study of the interaction between charged objects.

Coulomb's Law

$ \begin{eqnarray} F & = & \frac{q_{1}q_{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r^{2}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

where

  • $\epsilon_{0}$ is permittivity of free space ($8.85 \times 10^{-12} C^{2} / N \cdot m^{2}$)
  • $k = \Large \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}}$ is also known as Coulomb's constant.

The inclusion of $\pi$ is so that the constant appears in situations with spherical symmetry and cancels otherwise.

In vector form, Coulomb's Law is written

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{F}_{12} & = & \frac{q_{1}q_{2} (\Vector{r}_1 - \Vector{r}_2)}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} \Norm{\Vector{r}_1 - \Vector{r}_2}^{3}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Superposition

The total force due to a system of charges is the linear superposition of the force due to each charge.

Electric Field

In order to progress to a more abstract description of electrostatics, we introduce the concept of an electric field which is generated by charge sources. Why does introducing the field concept help? One perspective is that classical electrodynamics is fundamentally local. A charge experiences a force due to local fields.

The electric field is defined operationally as the force on a infinitesimal test charge.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{E} & = & \frac{1}{q_{test}} \Vector{F}_{test} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} E & = & \frac{Q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r^{2}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The electric fields is defined to be the force per unit charge.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{E}(\Vector{r}) & = & \frac{Q (\Vector{r} - \Primed{\Vector{r}})}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} \Norm{\Vector{r} - \Primed{\Vector{r}}}^{3}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Force on a charge in an Electric Field

We now begin to construct the electric part of the Lorentz force law.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{F} & = & q \Vector{E} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

We assume that the charge does not change the field significantly.

Conductors

Any excess charge on a conductor resides entirely on the outer surface.

The electrostatic field within the body of a conductor is zero.

For points outside a conducting sphere with excess charge, the electrc field behaves as if all the charge were at the center of the sphere.

The electric field is perpendicular to the surface, whatever the shape of the surface.

Electrical Potential Energy

The electrical potential energy of a system of charges is the work done on the system to assemble the charges by bringing each one from infinity.

$ \begin{eqnarray} U_{E} & = & \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \sum_{i<j} \frac{q_{i} q_{j}}{r_{ij}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Electric Potential due to a point Charge

Electrical potential energy per unit charge.

$ \begin{eqnarray} V & = & \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{Q}{r} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Units are J / C or the volt, $V$.

Flux

The total flux, $\Phi$, of a vector field, $\vec{v}$ through a surface with area $A \hat{n}$ is

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Phi & = & A \hat{n} \cdot \vec{v} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Gauss's Law

The flux of the electric field over a closed surface is $Q_{enclosed} / \epsilon_{0}$

$ \begin{eqnarray} \int_{S} \vec{E} \cdot \hat{n} \, dS & = & \frac{Q_{enc}}{\epsilon_{0}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The flux of $\vec{E}$ through a sphere due to a point charge, $q$, at the origin is easily seen to be $q / \epsilon_{0}$. A rigorous proof of Gauss's Law involves

  • Gauss's Theorem
  • $ \begin{eqnarray} \nabla \cdot (\vec{r} f(r)) & = & 3 f(r) + r \frac{d f}{d r} \\ \end{eqnarray} $
  • Construction of a connected region that excludes the origin.

Gaussian Surfaces

Surfaces with special symmetry provide a quick an easy way to compute electric fields.

  • Spheres
  • Planes
  • Cylinders

Electric Potential due to a Uniform Field

The electric field due to a large flat plate with uniform surface charge $\sigma$ is

$ \begin{eqnarray} E & = & \frac{\sigma}{2 \epsilon_{0}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

The electric potential a distance $d$ from the plate is

$ \begin{eqnarray} V(d) - V(0) & = & \frac{\sigma}{2 \epsilon_{0}} d \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Capacitance

Consider two parallel charged plates. Since it takes work to separate the charges, the capacitor is a storage device for electrical (potential) energy

$ \begin{eqnarray} E & = & \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_{0}} \\ \frac{\Delta V}{d} & = & \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_{0}} \\ \frac{Q}{\Delta V} & = & \frac{A \epsilon_{0}}{d} \\ C & = & \frac{Q}{\Delta V} \\ & = & \frac{A \epsilon_{0}}{d} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Energy Stored

$ \begin{eqnarray} W & = & \int_{0}^{Q} V dQ \\ & = & \int_{0}^{Q} \frac{Q}{C} dQ \\ & = & \frac{Q^{2}}{2C} \\ & = & \frac{1}{2} C V^{2} \\ & = & \frac{1}{2} Q V \\ \end{eqnarray} $

For a parallel plate capacitor, the energy density per unit volume between the plates is then

$ \begin{eqnarray} \rho_{E} & = & \frac{W}{A d} \\ & = & \frac{1}{2} C V^{2} \frac{1}{A d} \\ & = & \frac{1}{2} A \epsilon_{0} V^{2} \frac{1}{A d^{2}} \\ & = & \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_{0} E^{2} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Capacitors in Series

$V = V_{1} + V_{2}$, and $Q_{1} = Q_{2} = Q$, so

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{1}{C} & = & \frac{1}{C_{1}} + \frac{1}{C_{2}} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Capacitors in Parallel

$Q = Q_{1} + Q_{2}$, and $V_{1} = V_{2} = V$, so

$ \begin{eqnarray} C & = & C_{1} + C_{2} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Gauss's Theorem

This theorem provides the route to the differential formulation of Maxwell's 1st Equation.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \int_{V} d^3x (\nabla \cdot \Vector{A}) & = & \oint_{\partial V} \Vector{A} \cdot d \Vector{S} \end{eqnarray} $
