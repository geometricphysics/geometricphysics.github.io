Rigid Body Motion

Background

This is a traslation and interpretation of Max Born's 1910 paper, On the kinematics of the rigid body in the system of the principle of relativity.

Introduction

The rigid body of ordinary mechanics

Born starts from a definition of a rigid body in terms of the velocity of its points. In modern notation, if $\Vector{\omega}$ is the (axial) angular velocity vector, then the velocity $\Vector{v}_i$ of the $i$th point with position vector $\Vector{r}_i$ is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{v}_i & = & \Vector{\omega} \times \Vector{r}_i. \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Taking two arbitrary points, and forming the Galilean relative velocity we have

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{v}_i - \Vector{v}_j & = & \Vector{\omega} \times (\Vector{r}_i - \Vector{r}_j). \label{RelativeVelocity} \\ \end{eqnarray} $

He then shows that the separation, $|\Vector{r}_i - \Vector{r}_j|$, between any two points is a constant of the motion:

$ \begin{eqnarray} |\Vector{r}_i - \Vector{r}_j|^2 & = & (\Vector{r}_i - \Vector{r}_j) \cdot (\Vector{r}_i - \Vector{r}_j) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

Differentiating with respect to time and then using $\ref{RelativeVelocity}$, we find

$ \begin{eqnarray} |\Vector{r}_i - \Vector{r}_j| \frac{d |\Vector{r}_i - \Vector{r}_j|}{d t} & = & (\Vector{r}_i - \Vector{r}_j) \cdot (\Vector{v}_i - \Vector{v}_j) \\ & = & (\Vector{r}_i - \Vector{r}_j) \cdot (\Vector{\omega} \times (\Vector{r}_i - \Vector{r}_j)) \\ \end{eqnarray} $

From the definition of the cross product we must conclude

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d |\Vector{r}_i - \Vector{r}_j|}{d t} & = & 0. \end{eqnarray} $

Born warns us that we won't be able to transfer this conclusion to the relativistic domain, and we will gain an understanding of why.

Rest vectors of first and second kind

Born reminds us that a Lorentz transformation contains 6 independent parameters corresponding to a spatial rotation and the boost. He refers to boosts as special Lorentz transformations. He calls four-vectors vectors of the first kind, trnsforming like the coordinate differentials. He defines vectors of the second kind to be what we call an antisymmetric tensor of rank two. This has 6 independent components in spacetime, $f_{23}, f_{31}, f_{12}, f_{14}, f_{24}, f_{34}$.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Tensor{f} & = & \langle f_{23}, f_{31}, f_{12}, f_{14}, f_{24}, f_{34} \rangle \end{eqnarray} $

An antisymmetric second-rank tensor can be written in matrix form. Here I've used the symbol $\Box$ to indicate that the value is not independent i.e., $f_{ij} = - f_{ji}$. The diagonal values must be zero by antisymmetry.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Tensor{f} & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccccc} 0 & f_{12} & \Box & f_{14} \\ \Box & 0 & f_{23} & f_{24} \\ f_{31} & \Box & 0 & f_{34} \\ \Box & \Box & \Box & 0 \\ \end{array} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

Comparing this to the antisymmetric tensor that can be interpreted as a polar vector $\Vector{E}$ and an axial vector $\Vector{B}$, we see that Born's choice of components was designed to coincide.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Tensor{F} & = & \left[ \begin{array}{cccccc} 0 & B_{z} & \Box & -iE_{x} \\ \Box & 0 & B_{x} & -iE_{y} \\ B_{y} & \Box & 0 & -iE_{z} \\ \Box & \Box & \Box & 0 \\ \end{array} \right] \end{eqnarray} $

We see that in every reference system, the six components of $\Tensor{f}$ represent two space vectors, one polar and one axial.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{E} & = & \langle f_{14}, f_{24}, f_{34} \rangle \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{B} & = & i \langle f_{23}, f_{31}, f_{12} \rangle \end{eqnarray} $

Born defines the dual of the tensor $\Tensor{f}$ to be composed of the vectors in reverse order.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Dual{\Tensor{f}} & = & \langle f_{14}, f_{24}, f_{34}, f_{23}, f_{31}, f_{12} \rangle \end{eqnarray} $

Born now defines rest vectors as vectors whose fourth component vanishes. We consider two reference frames, $\Sigma$ and $\Primed{\Sigma}$, with parallel spatial coordinate axes, where the primed system has velocity $\Vector{v}$ relative to the unprimed system.
