Algebraic Geometry

An alternative approach to Euclidean geometry that empasizes coordinate proofs.

Point (definition)

A point $P= [x,y]$ is an ordered pair of numbers. The numbers $x$ and $y$ are the coordinates of $P$.

Proportion (definition)

For a natural number $n$, an $n$-proportion is an expression of the form

$ \begin{eqnarray} a_{1} : a_{2} : \cdots : a_{n} \end{eqnarray} $

where $a_{1}, a_{2}, \ldots, a_{n}$ are not all zero, such that

$ \begin{eqnarray} a_{1} : a_{2} : \cdots : a_{n} & = & \lambda a_{1} : \lambda a_{2} : \cdots : \lambda a_{n} \end{eqnarray} $

for any non-zero number $\lambda$.

Line (definition)

A line $L= \langle a : b : c \rangle$ is a 3-proportion, with the property that $a$ and $b$ are not both zero.

Notice that $a$ or $b$ can be zero, but not both at the same time.

Line through one Point (definition)

Let $P=[x,y]$, and $L = \langle a : b : c \rangle$, then

The phrase $P$ lies on $L$ or $L$ passes through $P$ means

$ \begin{eqnarray} a x + b y + c = 0 \end{eqnarray} $

Line through two Points (theorem)

For any two distinct points $P_{1}=[x_{1},y_{1}]$ and $P_{2} = [x_{2},y_{2}]$, there is a unique line $L = P_{1}P_{2}$, passing through them both given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} L = \langle y_{1} - y_{2} : x_{2} - x_{1} : x_{1} y_{2} - x_{2} y_{1} \rangle \end{eqnarray} $

The proof of this theorem is simply to solve the system of equations for two distinct points to lie on the same line.

Collinear Points (definition)

Three or more points which lie on a common line are collinear.

Collinear Points (theorem)

The points $[x_{1},y_{1}]$, $[x_{2},y_{2}]$, and $[x_{3},y_{3}]$ are collinear precisely when

$ \begin{eqnarray} x_{1} y_{2} - x_{1} y_{3} + x_{2} y_{3} - x_{2} y_{1} + x_{3} y_{1} - x_{3} y_{2} & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

The proof of this theorem involves first distinguishing whether the points are unique and then applying the theorem for a point lying on the line formed by the other two points.

Concurrent Lines (definition)

Three or more lines which pass through a common point are concurrent.

Concurrent Lines (theorem)

If the lines $\langle a_{1} : b_{1} : c_{1} \rangle$, $\langle a_{2} : b_{2} : c_{2} \rangle$, and $\langle a_{3} : b_{3} : c_{3} \rangle$ are concurrent then

$ \begin{eqnarray} \epsilon_{ijk} a_{i} b_{j} c_{k} & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} $

where $i,j,k = 1,2,3$ and $\epsilon_{ijk}$ is the Levi-Civita symbol.

This is the determinant of a matrix with columns $a$'s, $b$'s and $c$'s.

Isometry

A transformation $\sigma$ is an isometry means

  1. The transformation $\sigma$ is invertible.
  2. The transformation preserves the quadrance between any two points:

    $ \begin{eqnarray} Q(A_{1}, A_{2}) & = & Q(\sigma(A_{1}),\sigma(A_{2})) \\ \end{eqnarray} $
