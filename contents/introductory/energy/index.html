Energy

Kinetic Energy

The kinetic energy, $T$, of a particle with mass $m$ and velocity $\Vector{v}$ is defined to be

$T = \frac{1}{2} m \Vector{v}^{2}$.

Work

Work is the transfer of mechanical energy from one system to another.

Work is the reversible transfer of energy from one system to another.

When a force, $\Vector{F}$, does work on a particle by moving it through an infinitesimal displacement $d\Vector{x}$, the infinitesimal increase in energy of the particle is

$dW = \Vector{F} \cdot d\Vector{x}$.

Work-Energy Theorem

Having defined the kinetic energy of a particle and the work done by a force, we can show that the total work done by a force on a particle is equal to the change in kinetic energy of the particle. This is the work-energy theorem.

Proof.

Let $\mathbf{f}$ be the force on the particle of mass $m$ (assumed constant). The work done by $\mathbf{f}$ in moving the particle from a point $A$ to a point $B$ is

$ \begin{eqnarray*} W & = & \int_{A}^{B} dW \\ & = & \int_{A}^{B} \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}(t), t) \cdot d\mathbf{x} \\ & = & \int_{A}^{B} m \mathbf{a} \cdot d\mathbf{x} \\ & = & m \int_{A}^{B} \mathbf{\dot{v}} \cdot d\mathbf{x} \\ & = & m \int_{A}^{B} \mathbf{\dot{v}} \cdot \mathbf{v} dt \\ & = & \frac{1}{2} m \int_{A}^{B} \frac{d}{dt} (\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}) dt \\ & = & \frac{1}{2} m [\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}]_{A}^{B} \\ & = & T_{B} - T_{A}. \blacksquare \end{eqnarray*} $

Potential Energy

If a value is defined at every point in a region, then we say that a field exists in that region. The type of a general field might be specified as follows:

$f : (vector[L], vector[LT^{-1}], scalar[T]) \rightarrow vector[MLT^{-2}]$.

We will be interested in the case of a force field where the type of the value is a vector.

Suppose we compute the total work done in moving a particle around a closed path in the region where there exists a field $f$. We recognize that it will take a finite amount of time to move the particle.

$ \begin{eqnarray*} W = \oint_{t_{A}}^{t_{B}} \normalsize \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}(t), t) \cdot d\mathbf{x} \end{eqnarray*} $

If the field is steady, which is to say that $f$ does not depend explicitely on time,

$f : (vector[L]) \rightarrow vector[MLT^{-2}]$, then it is possible that the force does no net work once the particle returns to the starting point. In that case we have

$ \begin{eqnarray*} \oint_{t_{A}}^{t_{B}} \normalsize \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}(t)) \cdot d\mathbf{x} = 0 \end{eqnarray*} $

If we now split the integral into two parts where we go out to some intermediate point via one path and return to the starting point by a distinct path then we see that the work done in going from one point to another must be independent of the path taken.

$ \begin{eqnarray*} 0 & = & \oint \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) \cdot d\mathbf{x} \\ & = & \int_{A}^{B} \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x_{1}(t)}) \cdot d\mathbf{x} + \int_{B}^{A} \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x_{2}(t)}) \cdot d\mathbf{x}\\ & = & \int_{A}^{B} \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x_{1}(t)}) \cdot d\mathbf{x} - \int_{A}^{B} \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x_{2}(t)}) \cdot d\mathbf{x}\\ \end{eqnarray*} $

Therefore,

$ \begin{eqnarray*} \int_{A}^{B} \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x_{1}(t)}) \cdot d\mathbf{x} & = & \int_{A}^{B} \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x_{2}(t)}) \cdot d\mathbf{x}\\ \end{eqnarray*} $.

Since the work done is independent of the path then it can only depend on some function of the endpoints:

$ \begin{eqnarray*} \int_{A}^{B} \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x(t)}) \cdot d\mathbf{x} & = & - (V_{B} - V_{A}) \\ & = & - (V(\mathbf{b}) - V(\mathbf{a})) \\ \end{eqnarray*} $.

The minus sign is by convention and the reason for it will become clear.

So the path-independence of the work integral implies the existence of a scalar field $V$. The relationship between the scalar field $V$ and the force field $f$ can be found from the boundary theorem, also known as the fundamental theorem of calculus. Differentiating the work integral with respect to the upper limit we get

$ \begin{eqnarray*} \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{b}) \cdot d\mathbf{x} & = & - (V(\mathbf{b}+d\mathbf{x}) - V(\mathbf{a})) - (V(\mathbf{b}) - V(\mathbf{a})) \\ & = & - (V(\mathbf{b}+d\mathbf{x}) - V(\mathbf{b})) \\ & = & - \frac{\partial{V}}{\partial{x_{i}}} dx^{i} \\ & = & - \nabla{V}(\mathbf{b}) \cdot d\mathbf{x} \\ \end{eqnarray*} $.

But since the displacement $d\mathbf{x}$ is arbitrary, we must have

$ \begin{eqnarray*} \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{b}) & = & - \nabla{V}(\mathbf{b}) \\ \end{eqnarray*} $.

And since the endpoint $B$ is arbitrary, we find that

$ \begin{eqnarray*} \mathbf{f} & = & - \nabla{V} \\ \end{eqnarray*} $.

So when the net work done by a force in moving a particle around a closed circuit is zero, the force can be expressed as the (negative) gradient of a scalar field.
