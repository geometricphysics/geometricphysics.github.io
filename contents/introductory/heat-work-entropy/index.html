Work, Heat, and Entropy

Traditional Pedagogy

The following statements are typical of textbooks:

Heat and work are both descriptions of the transfer of energy.

Work is the mechanical transfer of energy (Knight) or a change in external parameters (Kittel).

Heat is the transfer of energy by thermal contact.

The fundamental difference is in the entropy transfer.

Conceptual Remarks

The use of the word mechanical is ambiguous when we consider that the transfer of energy at the inter-molecular level is also informally described as mechanical. We would like to have a terminology that helps us clarify the different ways in which we account for the energy transfer and how that causes us to consider the concept of entropy.

Randall suggests omitting entropy unless there is time to develop the probabilistic approach. He then keeps the second law in the form of Kelvin-Planck (impossibility of 100% efficiency) or Carnot (reversible engine has maximum efficiency).

First Law (Energy Conservation)

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Delta K + \Delta U + \Delta E_{int} & = & W + Q \end{eqnarray} $

Ideal Gas Equation

This must be introduced as an empirical law if it cannot be derived using statistical mechanics.

$ \begin{eqnarray} p V & = & n R T = N k_{B} T = N \tau \end{eqnarray} $

It might be interesting to observe that this is a statement about energy conservation for non-interacting particles constrained to a finite volume. The pressure $p$ is an energy per unit volume - an energy density. The scaling factor $k_{B}$ for the Kelvin temperature could be dropped if we move to natural units.

$C_{P} - C_{V} = R$ or $k_{B}$ or $1$ (depending on units).

Consider bringing a cylinder-piston-gas system at temperature $T_{i}$ into thermal contact with a resevoir at temperature $T_{f}$.

(1) Fix the piston position so that the process happens at constant volume.

$ \begin{eqnarray} Q & = & N C_{V} \Delta T \\ W & = & 0 \\ \Rightarrow \Delta E_{int} & = & W + Q = N C_{V} T \end{eqnarray} $

(2) Allow the piston to move freely so that the process happens at constant pressure.

$ \begin{eqnarray} Q & = & N C_{P} \Delta T \\ W & = & - \int_{V_i}^{V_f} p dV = - p \int_{V_i}^{V_f} dV = - p (V_f - V_i) = - N K_{B} (T_f - T_i) = - N K_{B} \Delta T \end{eqnarray} $

The key insight is that the internal energy at the end is the same in both cases because it depends only on temperature. This can be represented on a $pV$ diagram.

Adiabatic Compression

Consider the case when $Q = 0$ because of insulation or sufficiently fast change. We also make use of the fact that the internal energy only depends on the temperature.

$ \begin{eqnarray} W & = & - \int_{V_i}^{V_f} p dV = \Delta E_{int} = N C_{V} \Delta T \end{eqnarray} $

Assuming also that the change takes place slowly enough for internal equilibrium,

$ \begin{eqnarray} - p dV & = & N C_{V} dT \end{eqnarray} $

We now substitute for $p$, rearrange and integrate. The result is

$ \begin{eqnarray} T^{(\frac{C_V}{k_B})} V & = & const \end{eqnarray} $

or, using the ideal gas equation to eliminate $T$,

$ \begin{eqnarray} p V^{\gamma} & = & const \end{eqnarray} $

$ \begin{eqnarray} \gamma & = & \frac{C_P}{C_V} = 5/3 \end{eqnarray} $
