Momentum

Linear Momentum of a Particle

The linear momentum, $\Vector{p}$, of a particle with mass $m$ and velocity $\Vector{v}$ is defined to be

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{p} = m\Vector{v} \end{eqnarray} $

Newton's Second Law

The more general form of Newton's Second Law is

$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{d \Vector{p}}{dt} & = & \Vector{F} \label{Newton2} \end{eqnarray} $

If the mass of the particle is constant then

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Vector{a} & = & \frac{d \Vector{v}}{dt} & = & \frac{\Vector{F}}{m} \end{eqnarray} $

Momentum-Impulse Relationship

If we integrate $\ref{Newton2}$ then we obtain

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Delta \Vector{p} & = & \Vector{p}_f - \Vector{p}_i & = & \int_{t_i}^{t_f} dt \, \Vector{F}(t) \end{eqnarray} $

This quantity (on the right) is called the impulse of the force $\Vector{F}$ between $t_i$ and $t_f$.

In a graph of force versus time, the impulse of a force $\Vector{F}$ as it acts from $t_i$ to $t_f$ is the area under the graph bounded by the time limits. (You actually need three graphs in 3 dimensions).

Average Force

If a force $\Vector{F}(t)$ acts over an interval of time $\Delta t = t_f - t_i$, then the average force $\bar{\Vector{F}}$ is defined by

$ \begin{eqnarray} \bar{\Vector{F}} \Delta t & = & \int_{t_i}^{t_f} dt \, \Vector{F}(t) \end{eqnarray} $

Conservation of Linear Momentum

Newton's Third Law states that if one particle exerts a force on a second particle, then the first particle experiences a force that is equal and opposite to the force on the second particle.

I'm not too fond of the terminology 'action' and 'reaction' because it suggests a primacy or causality of one force over the other. I'd prefer to say that the forces of interaction between particles are pairwise and equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.

A consequence of Newton's Third Law is that In an isolated system, Linear Momentum is conserved.

The change in momentum of the $i$th particle due to the external force $\Vector{F}_i$ and the internal forces $\Vector{f}_{ij}$ is given by

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Delta \Vector{p}_i & = & \int_{t_{initial}}^{t_{final}} dt \, (\Vector{F}_i(t) + \sum_{j \ne i} \Vector{f}_{ij}(t)) \end{eqnarray} $

The change in momentum for the system of particles is obtained by summing over all particles.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Delta \Vector{P} & = & \sum_i \Delta \Vector{p}_i & = & \int_{t_{initial}}^{t_{final}} dt \, \sum_i (\Vector{F}_i(t) + \sum_{j \ne i} \Vector{f}_{ij}(t)) \end{eqnarray} $

As a consequence of Newton's Third Law, the contributions due to internal forces cancel in pairs.

$ \begin{eqnarray} \Delta \Vector{P} & = & \int_{t_{initial}}^{t_{final}} dt \, \sum_i \Vector{F}_i(t) & = & \int_{t_{initial}}^{t_{final}} dt \, \Vector{F}_{net}(t) \end{eqnarray} $

If the system is isolated then $\Vector{F}_{net}(t) = 0$, and so $\Delta \Vector{P} = 0$.

Angular Momentum of a Particle about a Point

The moment of momentum of a particle about a given point is called the angular momentum about that point.

If $\Vector{r}$ is the displacement from the point to the particle, and $\Vector{p}$ is the linear momentum of the particle, then the angular momentum, $L$, of the particle about the point is

$L = \Vector{r} \wedge \Vector{p}$
