The Photoelectric Effect

The Big Idea: Quanta

When light interacts with matter, energy is exchanged in discrete amounts called quanta. For a given frequency of light, $\nu$, the energy of the quantum is

$ E = h \nu $,

where $h$ is Planck's constant, determined experimentally.

Experiment and Observation

When light illuminates a metal, photoelectrons may be emitted and the maximim kinetic energy of the electrons varies as

$ K_{max} = h \nu - \phi $,

where $\phi$ is the work function of the metal.

The following phenomena are observed

  • The maximum kinetic energy depends on the frequency of the light source.
  • The is a lowest cutoff frequency below which no electrons are emitted.
  • The intensity of the light source only affects the number of electrons emitted.
  • The electrons are emitted almost instantaneously following illumination.

Classical Wave Theory Fails to Explain Phenomenon

This phenomenon cannot be explained by the classical, wave theory of light which would predict

  • Increasing the intensity of the light would increase the kinetic energy of the electrons.
  • Electrons would be emitted regardless of the frequency of the light.
  • There could be a significant time delay between illumination and ejection of electrons as electrons accumulate enough energy to escape the atom(s) to which they were bound.

Quantum Theory

The phenomenon is explained with the following assumptions

  • When light interacts with electrons the energy exchanged is quantized.
  • The quanta of light energy are called photons.
  • The energy of a photon is $E = h \nu$.
  • An photoelectron interacts with one quantum of light.
  • Energy is conserved
  • Some of the energy of the quantum goes into allowing the electron to escape the metal. This quantity of energy is characteristic of the metal and is called the work function, $\phi$.

Wave-Particle Duality?

It is often concluded that electromagnetic radiation behaves both like a particle and like a wave. If we consider that a particle is actually a point-like object with no size or internal structure then this conclusion starts to appear extreme and unfounded. While it is true that energy is exchanged in discrete amounts, this does not provide evidence that the photons are strongly localized in space as particles. The modern view is that photons are excitations of the electromagnetic field and have spatial extent.
